]]> By: Rand http://finkorswim.com/2012/04/30/analytic-thinking-can-undermine-belief-and-my-response/#comment-8000 Tue, 01 May 2012 03:25:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=5469#comment-8000 I should note that there is an actual very simple answer to your question.

“Is it reasonable to believe in something that correlates with lack of analytical thinking and that analytical thinking causes it to be disbelieved?”

Yes and yes. Just because something correlates with a lack of analytical thinking does not mean it is wrong. In fact this type of question strays dangerously close to the genetic fallacy: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_fallacy.

Likewise for part b, that analytical thinking causes it to be disbelieved. That’s irrelevant. If I have reason to believe something I’ll believe it on the basis of that merit. Just because other people believe it for different reasons shouldn’t concern me.

Consider the following the situation:

People are invited to participate in a lottery system. On the face of it, it seems that your expected gain from wagering $10 is approximately $9. 

You prime people in two different ways. The ones who you prime to think analytically will avoid wagering money, because they expect to lose. The ones who you prime with Nike commercials all go for it.

Now imagine I’m a mathematician, with a specialty in probability theory (and moreover, I actually have a good grasp of probability, which isn’t true of all specialists in the field, see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monty_Hall_problem.) Looking at how the lottery is structured, I recognize that the expected payoff of wagering $10 is actually $12. What should I do? I should wager! Yes, many people before went this route due to ignorance. That is the way the world works. That doesn’t effect my mathematical ability.

I’m surprised that many would view this study as problematic. I expect to vote in the Presidential Elections for the same candidate as roughly 50% of a populace that knows virtually nothing about politics. Does this invalidate my vote? Of course not. It is important to recognize ignorance, and even to therefore question your own knowledge. But at the end of the day you’re not all the other people. And you have a choice to make.

They function in a completely different way. It is beyond the scope of this post to propose what kind of thinking justifies religious belief.”  . You still haven’t explained what that is about, so there isn’t much further I can say on the matter now.

I took issue with your contention that analytic thought is not a threat to faith, because it is fairly clear to me that faith involves temporarily disabling or ignoring analytic thought. Hence the “two switches” metaphor. 

I didn’t consider the other side of the coin, which you’ve brought to my attention. Someone who is convinced, whether by Gerald Schroeder or simply an ignoramus who points out “the ridiculousness of evolution” that science argues for God, is unlikely to suddenly change his view when prompted to think analytically. In fact, analytic thinking should promote his religious belief, as he considers the impossibility of the universe without “the God of the Gaps.” 

It’s precisely the other type of individual, who doesn’t perceive belief in God as a consequence of rational analysis, who is likely to express more skepticism, when primed to think scientifically. Your alternative here seems closer to the default, and certainly the less safe of the options placed before us. Someone raised on Creationism, Young-Earthism and other forms of pseudo-science will not suddenly discover Carbon Dating and Natural Selection when primed to think scientifically, and his analytic thought will revolve around the sciences he has. However, when one leaps between the pillars of faith and the pillars of empiricism, if we lure him to the side of empiricism, he will be lured. And I think that’s precisely what this study shows.
