It won’t work. Setting people
up as opponents is exactly the wrong way to go about building tolerance. Also,
debating is not the way to arrive at the truth. Debating is a sport, and the
better debater wins, not necessarily the one who’s right.

> Further, most people are
not well versed enough in their religions to carry an honest debate.

In the theology and philosophy
of their religions. Most people just aren’t all that interested. Those who are tend
to become theologians or atheists.

> Learning philosophy and
engaging in debate in the more formative years of yeshiva learning could be
very beneficial. It was considered beneficial to the medieval Jewish
commentators.

In the middle ages,
only the brightest boys went to yeshiva, and there was much less in philosophy,
including natural philosophy, that challenged traditional religious views. I
agree that learning philosophy can be very beneficial, but for the average kid
it would either bore them silly or lead them to the sorts of questions I was
told to stop asking in high school because (to quote the rosh yeshiva) “The
other boys wouldn’t think of these questions, and it’s better their not
bothered by yours.”
