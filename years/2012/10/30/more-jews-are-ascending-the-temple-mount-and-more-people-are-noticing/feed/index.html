The difficulty is first and foremost with us.

There is an unholy alliance between Israel’s secular left wing and the Orthodox right wing. Each of them wants to keep Jews away from Har Habayit.

As you say almost all Orthodox Jews grew up thinking that setting foot anywhere in the area is a “chiuv kares”. It will take time to convince the Orthodox world that this is not true, and as Orthodoxy is so resistant to change this may be decades of time.

BTW – besides for Rabbi Tendler many Israeli Rabbis & Roshei Yeshiva from the Religious Zionist world have gone and continue to urge their students and followers to go as they consider it a mitzvah.

To stand any chance, we need to start focusing on Har Habayit. We need to put Har Habayit in the foreground and the Kotel in the background. At this point in history we do the opposite.

I myself have ascended Har Habayit al pi dat v’din (after tevilah etc.)
But even if you are in the halachik camp that does not go up to har habayit you can do things to keep har habayit in the proper perspective:

-when in israel, before going to the kotel, walk up to the ramp to the gate of har habayit and daven there.
– if you decorate your home with pictures of the kotel, then make sure to have pictures of har habayit.
-study it – there are some excellent new books about har habayit, including one in English by shaar press in conjunction w/the Western Wall Heritage foundation.
– visit the machon hamikdash in Jerusalem and explore the resources on their website (www.templeinstitute.org)

– if you hear ppl or read news stories that say “the western wall judaism holiest site” – correct them. Judaism’s holiest site (and only inherently holy site) is the Temple Mount.

the more we do to educate ourselves and to internalize the message that our real passion is for Har Habayit more so than the Kotel – the better chance we have of making headway, so that Har Habayit will not be the only place in the world that a Jew cannot pray.
