]]> By: Noam Greenberg http://finkorswim.com/2012/03/21/the-ny-times-ethicist-wants-to-know-why-you-eat-meat/#comment-7575 Thu, 22 Mar 2012 17:22:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=5300#comment-7575 I think part of it is how you frame the question, right? It seems as though you are starting from the assumption that it’s morally wrong to kill and eat an animal, but I think that assumption is incorrect, in and of itself.  Here’s why:

Killing for food is a biological imperative, built into nature and the food chain.  The fact is that animals from every size and shape eat other animals beneath them on the food chain (OK, not herbivores, but whatever).  It’s in every animals nature to either hunt and eat, or hide/defend and avoid being eaten.  It’s got nothing to do with be allowed to, it’s hardwired into nature.  

So if you start with the assumption that eating other animals is part of nature, you need a good reason to say it’s NOT allowed.  I don’t think you have that.  Sure, there are arguments you can make (humans have evolved into “higher” beings above animal instincts, for example) that are nice, but don’t rise to the level of ethically wrong. If you want to be an enlightened higher being, good for you. Reject your animal instincts and try to overcome them. But don’t say it’s immoral to live within them either.  Would you even ASK if it’s immoral to procreate? Of course not! It’s a biological imperative!

Is it wrong that humans have evolved to the top of the food chain? Does it change the morality that we’ve gotten very good at being on top of the food chain (farms, slaughterhouses, etc.) or does it change the morality that we’ve also gotten better at alternatives (farming, alternative protein sources, etc)? I say no. Morality/ethics are absolutes. If something is moral by nature, it’s moral now. It may make vegans uncomfortable, but it’s not unethical.

And that’s not even starting with the slightly off-balance question of whether you can even owe an ethical duty to an animal that doesn’t understand, doesn’t acknowledge and can’t reciprocate the same duty.

Here’s my last point on this: next time you encounter a hungry grizzly bear, please, by all means, talk to him about the ethics of killing/eating another animal. Hopefully, you’ll distract him long enough that I can run FAR away.
