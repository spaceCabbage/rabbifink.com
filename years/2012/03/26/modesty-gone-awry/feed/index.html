Eating disorders are widespread, and tend to be most prevalent, in fact, in cultures in which immodesty of dress is the norm. 

They are caused by a combination of psychological factors; there is no evidence that modest dress codes are a predisposing factor. Even the underlying attitude described as the animus behind Branhamism’s modesty codes, is one that is found in other cultures (Islam, for example), and has not been found to correlate with eating disorders.

]]> By: Agouradeb http://finkorswim.com/2012/03/26/modesty-gone-awry/#comment-7609 Mon, 26 Mar 2012 18:37:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=5308#comment-7609 As a therapist who treats eating disorders, I especially appreciate you pointing out the dangers of culturally and religiously imposed expectations that support unhealthy and disordered behaviors.  The orthodox community is not at all immune to eating disorders. Unfortunately the problem is pervasive enough that fortunately one of the leading treatment centers in the field has a program specifically for orthodox Jewish women.

However, there is a difference between disordered eating and eating disorders.  “Preparing” to date in the orthodox Jewish world encourages and supports disordered eating in the form of extreme dieting and exercising. This is not an eating disorder, though it can look like one at the time.

Professionally speaking, it would be much, much better and more effective for young women who feel that they need to lose weight to see a Registered Dietician–those who are trained to provide meal plans to help treat medical conditions (not a ‘nutritionist’ who might have an online certificate) or attend Weight Watchers in order to be at their most fit and confident before dating for shidduchim.  

Eating disorders, however, are medical conditions that are not caused by religion or culture or even family dynamics.  They pre-exist as neurological potentials that are triggered by stress (of religious, cultural and familial expectations, etc.).  And as medical conditions they require medical treatment.  Eating disorders can appear at any age (as young as 7 years old) and strike men as well as women  (10-15% of those with eating disorders are male) and effect up to 24 million people in the U.S.  

Sadly, eating disorders can be fatal.  Of all of the neurological disorders, eating disorders have the highest mortality rate.  Families and friends of those who are suffering cannot afford to look the other way. Neurological disorders are not cured, but they can be managed into more or less permanent remission.  Those with eating disorders need appropriate treatment to recover.

I recommend the National Eating Disorder Association (NEDA) and Anorexia Nervosa and Associated Disorders (ANAD) websites for more information and referrals for treatment.
