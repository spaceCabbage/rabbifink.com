Thank you. I have approached divrei Chazal like this for a while now. Why does this seem like such a chidush to many people? Chazal were intellectual and moral giants. In their times they adapted the Torah to make it a Toras Chaim, a relevant moral code. The great thinkers amongst the Jewish people have always done this (and are doing it today). That’s why today women do learn Torah and are considered to be able to contribute more than just sewing accumen. They were great then. Many wise men are great now.

There is however another approach which is still more accurate than the apologetic approach. Perhaps Chazal meant what they said, and were right! True it’s not PC but neither are korbanos, killing amalek, death penalty for shabbos desecration or many other things. Maybe women ideally should not learn. Maybe they can contribute more by providing family care than any other avenue. Maybe their emotions are too wishy washy to be able to think critically enough to decide moral, ethical or philosophical issues. Maybe even though a few women may break the mold, better that they would not fight nature and become compassionate nurturers instead. The irony is that my wife takes this position over mine! Olam hafuch raeesi! True, if this is the correct “Torah” approach then I may be in a moral dilemma of wanting to accept Torah but feeling that it is immoral. (I imagine our friend Tuvia may have had this issue at some point. He has decided by now). However, at least I can say that I am honest about what Torah is. (And after all, if one believes that it is from the Creator himself then it is ipso facto the “moral” approach).

The point is that when we learn Torah we have to try to understand what the navi, tana, amora, rishon, rav or rebbe is saying and only then think about how it fits in with what we think or believe. If we first formulate our opinions and then learn Torah, we will always try to apologetically explain everything and never honestly know what the text in question actually says! I’d rather be a scholar but struggle to relate to what I learn (even though it may create a crisis of faith) than be inspired by what I made up and pretended is an ancient Torah value.

Thanks again!
