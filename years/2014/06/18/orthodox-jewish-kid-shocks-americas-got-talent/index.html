Orthodox Jewish Kid Shocks America’s Got Talent

  • June 18, 2014
  • 150 comments
  • 0

It would be odd no matter the circumstances, but it was even odder considering our tense mood as we are so anxious to #bringbackourboys. A 12 year old Orthodox Jewish boy performed on Americas’ Got Talent last night. His talent is telling jokes. But he doesn’t tell the kind of jokes we would expect to hear from a nice Jewish boy. This was dirty. This kid didn’t even tell a joke that wasn’t dirty. It’s like he doesn’t even have any clean jokes.

The world witnessed Josh Orlian, a 12 year old boy, who is Jewish, who is Orthodox, who wore a yarmulka, who told dirty jokes, about his parents, with his parents present, tell dirty jokes and they laughed along with the audience. I felt dirty watching the clip. I won’t even link it here. It’s too dirty for this blog. But the judges loved it. The audience loved it too. Josh got through to the next round. Almost immediately, the emails and messages flooded my inboxes. Many people determined that this was a Chillul Hashem – a desecration of God’s name, and others were just plain abhorred and almost everyone was flabbergasted.

I was also pretty stunned after I saw the clip on Youtube, and it takes a lot to shock me. So I reached out to my comedy consultant and friend, Elon Gold for some insight.[You should watch his Netflix special if you are comfortable with an adult who might make a dirty joke every once in a while. It’s really funny and a little inappropriate.] I asked Elon if he had heard of this kid and it just so happens that their families are really good friends and Elon has even mentored Josh. In fact, Elon actually advised him to perform without the yarmulka because he felt it would be too distracting and possibly reflect poorly on Orthodox Judaism. But not only did Josh not take Elon’s advice, he wore a yarmulka from the bar mitzvah of Elon’s son! Now that’s pretty funny.

My first reaction to the performance was a toxic mixture of horror, fascination, and embarrassment. I was horrified that a kid was telling these jokes. I was even more horrified that he was being encouraged by his parents, the adult judges – including Heidi Klum who I am told through a mutual friend is a very wholesome mom, and the audience. I was horrified that this kid was Orthodox and wearing a yarmulka. Horrified. I was fascinated because I couldn’t understand how his parents were okay with these jokes. I was fascinated because this kid had instantly become a face of Orthodox Judaism for very non-Orthodox reasons. I was embarrassed because I am also Orthodox and I know that people associate Orthodox Jews as a monolithic group. But most of all, I was embarrassed that Orthodox Jewish schools, camps, shuls, youth groups, parents, teachers, friends, and rabbis, weren’t enough of an influence or inspiration in this kid’s life that he would think twice before making the entire country blush. We all bear some responsibility for this performance.

Even though initially Elon thought that Josh shouldn’t wear his yarmulka, after the audience and judges adored his performance, Elon felt that it wasn’t as bad as it could have been and it didn’t bother him as much. But many other people found it extremely distasteful and were embarrassed by his yarmulka. Elon pointed out that most of the non-Orthodox Jewish audience probably does not think negatively of Josh. They thought he was funny. And the fact that he is Orthodox only makes Orthodox Judaism look more normal in their eyes and that’s a good thing. In fact, all the non-Orthodox Jews who have shared this video thought it was great! I have not seen anyone non-Orthodox (aside from Youtube commenters, obviously) say anything negative about him or Orthodox Judaism due to his ribald performance.

I thought about it, and now I tend to agree. See, the problems that Orthodox Judaism faces today are less about the non-Jews thinking we are not religious enough or unfaithful to our tradition. I think most people get that part of who we are at this point. Our bigger problem is that as a group, Orthodox Judaism has a reputation as old, stodgy, boring, joyless, arcane, insular, and intolerant of others. So if we can somehow get people to figure out that we are committed to our faith and also not all those other things, I think the net result is a positive. The idea is that I think we need more work on our image of being normal than our image of being religious. If we accept that premise, it might be that Josh did us a favor by wearing his yarmulka on America’s Got Talent. I wouldn’t go so far as to say that it was a Kiddush Hashem and I wouldn’t recommend anyone do what Josh is doing, but in the aggregate it might balance out. At the very least, I think it might not be deserving of vitriol and disgust.

It’s also important to note that when people tell jokes with their friends it’s conversation between friends. It’s real. For a performer, it’s not conversation between friends. It’s not real. It’s theater. When Josh is on stage he is acting and playing a role. I’m not saying that this means he has carte blanche to say or do whatever pleases him with the excuse that he is just a performer. Judaism is a full time job and there are no breaks. But I think this might help people understand how people can view that as anything but abhorrent.

Ultimately, Josh has a pretty awesome opportunity to show the world that Orthodox Judaism is not defined by its stereotypes and we have all kinds of Orthodox Jews. That’s also a good thing. It’s good for others to see and it’s good for us to see as well. I think we should be rooting for Josh to succeed and I hope he does well.

There’s one more important lesson here. The Orthodox Jews who are horrified by this performance do not want to be judged by others because of Josh. They do not want people to think that there is any association or meaningful relationship between Josh and other Orthodox Jews. That’s a normal feeling to have when we see someone from our group doing something we find unsavory. People tend to want to disassociate from the objectionable person. I don’t love that attitude, but I am okay with it. I understand why people have a need to distance themselves from certain people. But we have to realize that we tend to do the same thing to other groups. We disapprove of certain individuals from specific groups for their abhorrent behavior and are often guilty of assuming that everyone in the group approves of that person or that they are all the same. We don’t want others to do it to us. We should not do it to others.

    If I get it, a major point he is making is that general audiences can now understand better that there are different types of Orthodox Jews out there (just as there’s diversity and individuality within any ethnic, societal, or religious group). Sure the talent act had a serious downside, he stresses, but the few jokes by a child (who’s attended the yeshiva day school system) can yet have some redeeming value: “Josh did us a favor” by showing that we can be committed Jews and still normal (not just relics of our Ancient Near East origins).

    I’d imagine some readers may be willing to accept this thesis, or at least in part, but I think Rabbi Fink makes it a tougher swallow by including a key sentence in the same paragraph:

    “The idea is that I think we need more work on our image of being normal than our image of being religious.”

    There it is again. It’s either-or. We so often feel the need to separate the words. You either show your religious self or your normal self.

    Until Orthodox educators (and columnists) find a way to merge the two, to impart that one can indeed present their normal and religious self concurrently – on the ball field, in the supermarket, at the office, during a Passover program on some Caribbean island – and on national TV (even in a comedy routine!) – then we have a level of educational failure that shares a large part of the blame for what any well-meaning child and his parents might partake in, whether it becomes the talk of news outlets and social media or remains a private affair.
