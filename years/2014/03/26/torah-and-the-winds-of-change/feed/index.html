These examples show that pesaq can change as social realities change the facts on the ground. Not that pesaq changes to conform to them. Similarly, the launching of the Beis Yaakov movement wasn’t to accommodate a desire to study because the zietgeist pushed toward women studying. The girls themselves were content to assimilate. Rather, the new social reality of universal secular education for girls meant that the old ruling didn’t fit the new reality. The idea of teaching girls enough so that they can grow up into observant women shifted from only including a few basics and practical day-to-day halakhah to including enough to keep them engaged and wanting to stay observant.

The second difference is that you’ll note that all these inventions either came from the minds of the leading posqim of the generation or (as in the case of Beis Yaacov) validated by them. Which was R’ Twersky’s and R’ Schachter’s primary thesis: Knowing when you’re applying Torah values with the greatest fidelity possible to a new situation, and when one is conforming to external values at the expense of the Torah’s requires more knowledge and more experiential immersion in the flow of halakhah (shimush) than a typical local rabbi achieves.
