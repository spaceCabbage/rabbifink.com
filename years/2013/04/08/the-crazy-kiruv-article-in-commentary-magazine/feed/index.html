My family’s traditional Conservative shul was quite good at producing a number of kids who grew up to be observant and engaged Jews – but many of them are not doing so within the Conservative movement. I would agree with a previous poster, though, that while some did go on to be full-fledged black-hat BTs, many chose to lead Conservadox lives within Orthodox or Chabad settings. For example, I know tons of families from my Chabad shul who send their children to Camp Ramah.

I’m wondering if Chabad and some other kiruv groups are actually creating a new way of Judaism altogether, as opposed to simply trying to persuade Jews to become Orthodox. There’s plenty of attraction to programs that reach and out serve all Jews, where all are welcome regardless of current level of observance, dress, etc. At some point, does this open approach become a value in itself, as opposed to just being a means to an end?

As for cannibalism other movements – I think that competition can be an impetus to change and discard ineffective practices. The old model where congregations could assume that families felt compelled to make a Bar Mitzvah and could therefore strong-arm those families into a few years of synagogue membership and Hebrew school did not produce committed families. Instead of grumbling, it would serve the non-Orthodox movements better to look at what Chabad and other kiruv groups do well – and do it better, while pointing out what unique points they have to offer.

]]> By: Philo http://finkorswim.com/2013/04/08/the-crazy-kiruv-article-in-commentary-magazine/#comment-12103 Mon, 08 Apr 2013 17:56:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=6894#comment-12103 “One thing that seemed improbable to me was the assertion that most kiruv success stories are formerly conservative Jews. My experience has been the complete opposite. That is, the less one knows about Judaism, the more likely one is to become frum.”

Without demographic surveys, our evidence is all anecdotal. But I think it depends on the setting.

Perhaps in Yeshivish kiruv, the unaffiliated Jews form a plurality of “baalei teshuva”. I don’t know. Also, could your experience be colored by the people who impulsively stop in at the PJC while walking on the boardwalk? That’s probably not a typical scenario.

In my personal experience, a huge number of Orthodox Jews I know, including my wife, come from engaged Conservative backgrounds. For those people, it’s less a result of a kiruv operatives, but rather a social adjustment, as they simply hang out with a more Orthodox group. This is very common in singles communities like the Upper West Side, where there is a large part of the Shamor Shabbat spectrum straddles the line between Orthodox and Conservative. Thus, on Shabbat mornings, people attend all sorts of different shuls; Modern Orthodox, Conservative, or independent minyanim like Hadar and Dakhei Noam, which occupy various parts of the space in between. Then those singles will join together for lunch. Often those singles are even roommates, as with a friend of mine, who was firmly Modern Orthodox, and shared an apartment with his firmly Conservative roommate.

With that kind of mixing, which I suspect is similar on many college campusus, it’s inevitable that people will flow from one side towards the other (and vice versa, for that matter).

So I think that Wertheimer may be right about a huge number of Conservative Jews ending up Orthodox, but I’m not sure that he’s right that their Orthodoxy is a result of organized kiruv efforts.
