It goes to starting with the assumption that outright philosophical statements should be first approached as agreeing, and only when that fails do we conclude we’re dealing with a dispute.

So you need to not only deal with whether or not to reconcile the stories (for which your conclusion seems inevitable) but also whether or not the message each story imparts can be reconciled. Because of the latter, conflicts between stories do usually pose a challenge, since it’s rare that both narratives can fit the same message even while the stories themselves couldn’t coexist in a single history.

]]> By: R Micha Berger http://finkorswim.com/2013/10/21/how-the-torah-temimah-deals-with-conflicting-midrashim/#comment-14454 R Micha Berger Mon, 21 Oct 2013 21:00:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=7729#comment-14454 You lost me here: “The easy answer is that it’s not a question because Midrashim don’t have to reconcile. … But the Torah Temimah does not answer it that way. Instead he attempts to show that there were two competing theories in Chazal…”

In the first clause, on what level are you saying they don’t have to reconcile: on the story level or the message level? Here, and in nearly all cases, the conflict goes beyond the metaphor and the two different versions of the story do have differences in message.

And if we’re saying that two midrashim don’t have to reconcile even in message, then isn’t that because of dispute between their authors? That, like Ethan Cohen wrote (at around 1:20 PST), it’s no less possible to have a dispute (machloqes) in Jewish Thought as we have in halakhah? In fact it’s more probable, as on most issues the rules of pesaq (finalizing a halachic decision) have no parallel for medrash.

So all-in-all, I’m, not sure how “Midrashim don’t have to reconcile” is different than “there were two competing theories in Chazal”.
