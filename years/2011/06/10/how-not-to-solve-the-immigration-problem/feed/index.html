It doesn’t solve the immigration problem, nor do I think it claims to solve it. Heck, it might even make it worse, folks with kids may register their kids as children of other people in the country legally which could lead to a future paperwork nightmare.

the bill gives state officials permission to question the citizenship of anyone in the entire state when there is “reasonable suspicion” that the person is here illegally.

Let’s assume it is improper for the state to do this. When do you think it is proper for the state to verify citizenship? And why (i.e. which section of law permits the cases of proper verification?)

I am not an expert, but I’m pretty sure this is in conflict with established Supreme Court precedent requiring that states not make citizenship a burden on education.

I doubt the law says much about “citizenship” per se, it probably talks more about being present in the country legally or not. Is a state legally required to educate kids that are in the country illegally? Which law provides for this requirement?

and requires all employers use the Federal E-Verify system before hiring any workers.

These kinds of laws have already created paperwork messes because undocumented residents simply obtain a false SSN and use it. Sometimes tens or hundreds of employees use the same number!

1. Clearly this is a far reaching bill that makes the government more powerful. I have become so accustomed to hearing that conservatives want to limit governmental intrusion. This seems to go contrary to that position.

What do you mean by “the government”? Generally the concern is with the Federal government becoming too powerful and usurping the rights of the states to differ from one another. However, in the larger sense I agree that all levels of government have become too powerful (and intertwined to a much greater degree than any of us should be comfortable with)

2) Much more importantly, this is a bill that would allow law enforcement to profile and subject anyone who looks like they are here illegally to prove their legality. There is no way that this law will not be discriminatory and degrading. There is no way to determine if someone “looks illegal” without tapping into harsh and ignorant stereotypes.

Depends on how you define “discriminatory”. If you define it as stopping Hispanic-looking folks much more than others, then yes. But if you define it as stopping folks in approximately the same percentages as they remain in the country illegally, then perhaps not. Also, I doubt the law would be implemented in such a haphazard (and very inefficient) manner of simply stopping random people on the street. More likely they would target workplaces that are known to hire illegals (or after work hangouts where illegals are known to congregate). But I agree that it is unfair to Hispanic-looking people. It’s also unfair that Hispanic-looking people make up the vast majority of people in our country illegally.

Agree with the law?

Not really. I think it’s a political stunt borne out of frustration with DC not doing enough (not really doing anything effective) to solve the illegal immigration problem. Ironically, defending the borders is one of the things the Federal government is supposed to be doing, and doesn’t do very well, yet they (the Federal government) is more than willing to stick their nose into all sorts of other things that aren’t really within their mandate (education and healthcare for example).

Thoughts on immigration?

It seems as if we are on a path to provide amnesty (and citizenship) to many of the folks here illegally. I have many problems with this, but it’s almost inevitable. The USA is not a country that can have 2 classes of people living here multi-generationally, that’s just not the way we roll. However there will be grave ramifications if Asian and European folks that desperately want to immigrate to the USA learn that the best way to get here is to cross the border legally, and then remain illegally for as long as it takes to acquire amnesty.
