A Response to Dennis Prager: The Real Reasons University Graduates Are Left Wing and / or Secular

  • April 8, 2011
  • 20 comments
  • 0

I respect Dennis Prager. Often, I vehemently agree with him. Sometime I do not.

Last week I saw an article by Dennis Prager that I took issue with. But the topic has been already discussed on this blog so I decided to let it go and not post a response. This week, another Dennis Prager article caught my attention. It also caught the attention of a writer at The Atlantic.

In an article on the National Review Online called, God Isn’t Doing Well, Dennis Prager posits some interesting food for thought about the quality and quantity of belief in God and religion in America. According to Prager, God isn’t doing very well. I’m not sure where he gets that from, but he seems pretty sure about it. I don’t know if I agree with his assumption, but for the sake of this blog post we will assume he is right. Again, he might be completely wrong about that point. I just don’t know.

Prager blames the universities for the secularization of America. But not in the way I would have thought. He accuses the universities of a sneaky secular agenda that seeks to produce left-wing secularists. He proves this point by stating what I believe to be a fact, that the more education one receives, the more likely they are to hold secular and left wing views. I reject the idea that these two things are interchangeable. Secularism is different than liberalism. God can be doing just fine even if university graduates are becoming left wing. A victory for the left wing is not necessarily a loss for God. More on that later.

First, we will explore how and why university graduates become left wing and or lose their religion. Prager dismisses the straw man argument that he presents saying that University education makes people think more clearly, thus producing left-wingers, as false because of all the people he speaks to on the radio. They don’t seem very intelligent when they call his show, so the educational system cannot be producing intelligent clear thinkers. Again, I don’t think this is a very compelling argument. His sample size is obviously small. They are the people listening to him and of the listeners, they are the ones who call him, and of the ones who call him they are the ones he asks about their education. Certainly it is not a fair sample size to dismiss university education as producing clear thinkers. Be that as it may, I think he is wrong on far more egregious accounts. There is a much more reasonable explanation for college students losing their religion.

In The Atlantic,Conor Friedersdorf writes just what I was thinking. For most people, religion is social (or driven by parental pressure). Your family celebrates certain holidays, goes to a specific house of worship, you live in an area with like-minded people and your social circle is dominated by those people who celebrate the same holidays, attend the same houses of worship and share your beliefs. It is easy to be religious under those circumstances. So easy, that one can coast along without consideration for one’s beliefs. It almost seems impossible to believe anything else.

At college, those existing social ties are broken. The umbilical cord is cut. Young men and women meet hundreds and thousands of people who think differently, look different, act different and believe differently. For the first, time, many people are challenged in their beliefs. They wonder why they do X and their friends do Y and Z. They see the world as a collection of many possibilities instead of one absolute reality that was being confirmed by like minded people back home.

When that happens, people question their beliefs. They question why they were doing what they were doing. Sometimes they don’t have any good answers. Without answers, their beliefs will disappear.

Is that a secret secular academic agenda? I don’t think so. Is it inherently evil? I don’t think so.

Friedersdorf addresses God and religion. I would like to add one more point to Friesendorf’s idea and address liberalism. When we live among like-minded people it is easy to forget that there are lots of folks who are different from us. Seeing and meeting people from other backgrounds and ethnicities can open one’s mind to the various challenges, social problems, biases and unfairness that those others deal with. If you become their friends it is only natural to become concerned with their well-being. Social equality and helping those with disadvantages is a cornerstone of left wing beliefs. It is much easier to ignore people who are facing inequalities when you don’t know them or even about them. But once they become part of one’s social network it is entirely normal to be drawn toward their cause.

Again, is this a secretsecular academic agenda? I don’t think so. Is it inherently evil? I don’t think so.

Further, residents of big cities, especially on the coasts, are also more likely to be left wing. Is there a secret secular agenda drug in the water? No. It is the same theory. When you meet people from diverse backgrounds, social, political and economic equality become a relevant issue for many people. This, I believe, migrates them towards the left.

Prager makes a few other points in his article that are worth mentioning.

He blames the clergy for being too soft on their congregants. All I can say is that orthodox Jewish clergy and teachers are very demanding and Jewish law does require built in models of respect and reverence for teachers. I believe these are good values. I am not sure if theyare related to the issue of the article. But they are good values nevertheless.

Another reason Prager mentions in the article is the inability to deal with pain and suffering. Again, I am not sure that people are drawn away from God because of this, but he is right on target here about the failures at dealing with is. Theodicy has been a blunt tool for polemicists for ages and I think most people are uncomfortable with the idea that a man can know what God “intends” when disaster strikes. Pastors and rabbis are guilty of this and Prager is wrong when he says that the right wing elements of religions are better equipped to dealing with disaster than the more liberal sides. I don’t think either side is better equipped than the other. There are morons on both sides. Strident admonishments from televangelistsabout the wrath of God striking Haiti or Japan can’t be the solution that Prager is suggesting “works”. So I don’t quite know where he is going with this point.

Finally Prager blames the tepid response of liberal churches and synagogues to Islamic fundamentalism. I am not sure if the tepid response is to blame as much as the acts of cowardice and violence made in the name of God by radical Muslims. When people see the level of atrocity capable in the name of God, I believe, it is a turn off. Though I don’t know how much we need to have an “untepid” response as much as we need to reinforce the idea of our versions of God do not allow for such militant violence.

Returning to the substantive point in this post, we are all influenced by our environements. Rambam writes that people are drawn to the ideas of their surroundings. When the environment is closed and controlled, our beliefs are confirmed, unchallenged and easy to keep. This has advantages and disadvantages. When we venture out of our homes we encounter new ideas, new people and new challenges. How we deal with those ideas, people and challenges will determine the level of Godliness we integrate into our lives. It is our job to train ourselves and our children and our students to deal with the issues they will encounter.

I also believe it is important not to conflate political ideology and religious beliefs. There are plenty of left wing people who are religious and plenty of right wing people whose religious beliefs are flimsy or clearly erroneous. Politics and religion are meant to be two separate things. That’s the way the Torah system was in ancient Israel. The priests and the monarchs were from different families. And that’s the way it should be in America as well.

  • Anonymous

    A very well-reasoned and well-articulated article. I’d like to address two arguments that do not relate directly to the articles main thesis, but are very interesting nonetheless.

    Quote: ‘I am not sure if the tepid response is to blame as much as the acts of cowardice and violence made in the name of God by radical Muslims. When people see the level of atrocity capable in the name of God, I believe, it is a turn off. Though I dont know how much we need to have an untepid response as much as we need to reinforce the idea of our versions of God do not allow for such militant violence.’

    Aside from the obvious note that “cowardice” (OED: “want of courage to face danger”) is not particularly apropos, I think this statement ignores an important point. Many religious systems, and very arguably* “Orthodox Judaism” among them, claim to be morally self-contained. I think this makes intuitive sense. I have difficulty enough arguing for the existence of any single moral system (the is-ought problem) that I’d be hesitant to multiply my problems by claiming that there is a God driven morality in addition to an independent kind. In this light, the actions of fundamentalists appear consistent and correct, within their moral system. (Read “within their equally justified moral system,” assuming that basic morality must always be an axiom within its own system.) I see no difficulty with recognizing that they are acting in accordance with their beliefs while insisting that they all be locked up, for the good of society.

    Should Orthodoxy “reinforce the idea of our versions of God do not allow for such militant violence?” Certainly the religion upon which it is based did allow for such violence, in the cases of religious wars and the slaughter of Amalek, or Ir Hanidachat and the many religious violations punished by death. Perhaps, given the extent to which Orthodox Judaism clings to its old traditions, it should emphasize that it represents an “authentic self-contained system”, in contrast to other “watered-down” religions and denominations. Naturally this would be a “turn off” to a good humanist, but perhaps Orthodox Judaism shouldn’t be tailoring its message to humanists.

    This brings me to my next objection:
    Quote: “Theodicy has been a blunt tool for polemicists for ages and I think most people are uncomfortable with the idea that a man can know what God intends when disaster strikes. Pastors and rabbis are guilty of this and Prager is wrong when he says that the right wing elements of religions are better equipped to dealing with disaster than the more liberal sides. I dont think either side is better equipped than the other. There are morons on both sides. Strident admonishments from televangelists about the wrath of God striking Haiti or Japan cant be the solution that Prager is suggesting works. So I dont quite know where he is going with this point.”

    Every monotheistic religion is predicated upon a simple claim: Man *can* know the will of God. Without that claim, no one would claim to “practice” a religion, he would claim to know that God exists, a piece of information no more relevant to his life than the Czech word for watermelon. Moses, Jesus, Mohammed and Joseph Smith Jr. all claimed to know God’s will very well, and to see it not merely in his words but in his actions. This pushes many of their followers to make one very dubious claim, that God can violate the perceived rules of nature. But once they’ve made that claim, is it worthwhile to throw it out to avoid seeming like the televangelists? Consider that now they have to jump through another hoop, and claim that God has gone from being administrator of the universe (clearly reflecting almost all early religious beliefs) to merely its creator. They must reject all prayer for God help, or create convoluted explanations for how they serve a different goal than their contents imply.

    Even once they’ve jumped through these hoops, religious revisionists still face a problem. They still need to learn the will of God to remain religious rather than simply Deists. Are they better off learning God’s will from a tsunami thousands of years ago or from a typo? The concept of an immanent God gives relevance to people’s lives and transforms religion from people talking towards a wall to real, personal interactions, with perceptible effect upon human lives. Humility may prompt a shift towards a less personal God, but I’m not convinced that it makes religion more appealing or, (presumably more importantly for the average religious person) that it solves any of the conundrums that religion must deal with in the first place.

    (Note: This should in no way imply that picketing funerals, blaming someone for their loss, or any of the other garbage people engage in, is acceptable. Some measure of common sense, combined with recognition of the fairly universal rule in all moral systems that you don’t cause people unnecessary anguish (“al tehiye scumbag”), should be enough to prevent these kinds of actions. Providence doesn’t have to imply being a jerk.)

    *There is quite a bit in Jewish tradition to argue to sides of this point, I’ve merely skimmed the surface. I merely wanted to respond to the latent assumption in the paragraph, that all forms of Islam must adhere to some established morality, and that Orthodox Judaism obviously does so.

    Prager is an intellectual lightweight. I’m surprised you waste your time giving him the time of day.

Now, after of 30 years of living in an Orthodox neighborhood, being very active in my Conservative shul, and seeing what my fellow Jews are up to, I’m ready to ditch it all, get an apartment in Greenwich Village or San Fransisco, or Paris, or something and be mostly secular. That’s what happens when you occasionally daven in black-hatter shuls and read Orthodox blogs (or at least the Orthodox commenters on DovBear.)

    I can’t be an atheist, because it’s impossible to prove a negative. That doesn’t mean that I have to believe in the truth claims of Orthodox Judaism, though. Of course, I can’t prove that Orthodox Judaism is false, either, but I have enough evidence to suggest to me that the truth claims of its theology are very unlikely to be true. Which means that unless I get some sort of other material or psychic benefit from participating in the Orthodox community, why should I bother? (By the way, the same applies to Conservative and Reform Judaism as well.) I participate in a religious community because of the fellowship, the rituals provide me some grounding and peace of mind, and the ethical principals (most of which are shared with other religious faiths) provide a better life for all. God plays no part in this, except at a theoretical level. When God talks to me, I suppose I’ll listen. When a rabbi talks, I might listen, but skeptically. And this is only right. Surely rabbis, even gedolim and even chazal, don’t presume to be God.
