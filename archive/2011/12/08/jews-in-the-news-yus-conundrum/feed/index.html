]]> By: Shlomo Pill http://finkorswim.com/2011/12/08/jews-in-the-news-yus-conundrum/#comment-6594 Shlomo Pill Fri, 09 Dec 2011 07:07:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=4897#comment-6594 Well said.  Unfortunately, the way many of the commentators framed the issue, it became a contest between Torah values and American/Western/Constitutional values.  If the question is framed that way, there can only be one answer – at least if YU want’s to keep that “Y.”  Perhaps it would have been more productive to take the triumph of the Torah value – tznius, nivul peh, arayos, what have you – as a given, and then debate whether the article did in fact breech any inviolable Torah barriers.  Within the context of that discussion it would have been also useful to distinguish between (1) the message of the article, (2) the way that message was expressed, (3) the Beacon’s decision to relay that message through this medium (as apposed to the far more objective and intellectual way another student argued for franker discussion about real sexual issues in a previous edition of the Beacon), (4) the fact that the article was not placed to express a message at all, but as a creative writing piece.

Framed this way, the issue becomes a halachic question, or at least a Torah-policy question, that could perhaps have been answered by YU’s Torah authorities in a sympathetic, understanding, and nuanced way.  (I hope for too much, I’m sure).

In the end, the fact that the discussion was largely not premised on the absolute triumph of Torah values, but instead entertained the possibility that Free Speech in a university should control, is troubling and really raises the question about where YU is as a Torah Umadda (or any other Torah-and-something) institution.

]]> By: Shlomo Abraham http://finkorswim.com/2011/12/08/jews-in-the-news-yus-conundrum/#comment-6592 Shlomo Abraham Fri, 09 Dec 2011 06:57:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=4897#comment-6592 I went to YU for a year (1999/2000), the episode seems rather reflective of my experience. I used to say at the time that 80% of the student body didn’t want to be there: 40% would rather be at Columbia, and another 40% would rather be at NIRC. The combined 80% didn’t really buy into Torah U’Mada, and rather view it as some sort of compromise.

A similar situation happened during my year: A gay student ran for a student council position (I believe VP): He had two opponents who were on the frummer side of things. During the week before the election, rabbanim in shiur encouraged students to not vote for “people whose values didn’t reflect the school”, and one of the two opponents was basically forced to drop out to not split the vote. In the end, he did not win, but refused to make a stink out of it. He said he liked the school, and knew that media attention would reflect poorly on YU.

Viewing the Beacon story in a simple what’s positive/negative is rather simplistic: It cuts to the core problems of the YU: A fundamental gap between the school’s philosophy and (portions of) the student body. This sort of thing would never happen in say BYU: Students buy in to Mormonism, make their pledges, keep their pledges, or they leave. Contrast that with YU where you won’t have a hard time finding students who don’t really buy into Torah, perhaps fewer who don’t buy into Mada, and very very few students who buy into the Soleveichik Torah/Mada enhance each other philosophy.

Perhaps you should address how/why YU came to have such a gap, whether it’s a problem, and how should they go moving forward.
