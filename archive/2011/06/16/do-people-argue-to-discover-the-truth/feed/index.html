]]> By: I Tick http://finkorswim.com/2011/06/16/do-people-argue-to-discover-the-truth/#comment-5657 Fri, 17 Jun 2011 00:15:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=4361#comment-5657 I’m not certain that the Talmud doesn’t function according to your characterization of the law. In many cases, it seems the Talmud is concerned with implementing law as a policy, based as much as possible on the truth, but ultimately relying on a series of requirements for proof, just like in a legal case. Rav X’s statement has a source in Scripture or in a Beraisa, but Rav Y’s does not, or Rav Y’s Scriptural proof is co-opted by Rav X for a different purpose, and the Sages like that usage more, because if better fulfills some other requirement, or makes a for a more cogent argument according to common reason, etc.

The search for truth is out there, and of course formal standards and common sense creep into any search for truth, but the legal system generally does conform to some sort of “show” where the arbiters must judge how well a case is made, usually according to some accepted conditions for proof or rationale established by precedent.
