His complaint seems to be about a lack of “godliness” (whatever that is) in people who go to the university. This is, of course, the exact opposite of my life experience. When I was attending college, I was quite religious, I even thought about becoming Orthodox. I suspect this may have been a way to assert my Jewish identity in the face of being in a diverse college community where Jews were a smaller minority than they had been in my high school. Now, after of 30 years of living in an Orthodox neighborhood, being very active in my Conservative shul, and seeing what my fellow Jews are up to, I’m ready to ditch it all, get an apartment in Greenwich Village or San Fransisco, or Paris, or something and be mostly secular. That’s what happens when you occasionally daven in black-hatter shuls and read Orthodox blogs (or at least the Orthodox commenters on DovBear.)

I can’t be an atheist, because it’s impossible to prove a negative. That doesn’t mean that I have to believe in the truth claims of Orthodox Judaism, though. Of course, I can’t prove that Orthodox Judaism is false, either, but I have enough evidence to suggest to me that the truth claims of its theology are very unlikely to be true. Which means that unless I get some sort of other material or psychic benefit from participating in the Orthodox community, why should I bother? (By the way, the same applies to Conservative and Reform Judaism as well.) I participate in a religious community because of the fellowship, the rituals provide me some grounding and peace of mind, and the ethical principals (most of which are shared with other religious faiths) provide a better life for all. God plays no part in this, except at a theoretical level. When God talks to me, I suppose I’ll listen. When a rabbi talks, I might listen, but skeptically. And this is only right. Surely rabbis, even gedolim and even chazal, don’t presume to be God.

]]> By: Anonymous http://finkorswim.com/2011/04/08/a-response-to-dennis-prager-the-real-reasons-university-graduates-are-left-wing-and-or-secular/#comment-5063 Fri, 08 Apr 2011 19:22:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=4100#comment-5063 A very well-reasoned and well-articulated article. I’d like to address two arguments that do not relate directly to the articles main thesis, but are very interesting nonetheless.

Quote: ‘I am not sure if the tepid response is to blame as much as the acts of cowardice and violence made in the name of God by radical Muslims. When people see the level of atrocity capable in the name of God, I believe, it is a turn off. Though I don’t know how much we need to have an “untepid” response as much as we need to reinforce the idea of our versions of God do not allow for such militant violence.’

Aside from the obvious note that “cowardice” (OED: “want of courage to face danger”) is not particularly apropos, I think this statement ignores an important point. Many religious systems, and very arguably* “Orthodox Judaism” among them, claim to be morally self-contained. I think this makes intuitive sense. I have difficulty enough arguing for the existence of any single moral system (the is-ought problem) that I’d be hesitant to multiply my problems by claiming that there is a God driven morality in addition to an independent kind. In this light, the actions of fundamentalists appear consistent and correct, within their moral system. (Read “within their equally justified moral system,” assuming that basic morality must always be an axiom within its own system.) I see no difficulty with recognizing that they are acting in accordance with their beliefs while insisting that they all be locked up, for the good of society.

Should Orthodoxy “reinforce the idea of our versions of God do not allow for such militant violence?” Certainly the religion upon which it is based did allow for such violence, in the cases of religious wars and the slaughter of Amalek, or Ir Hanidachat and the many religious violations punished by death. Perhaps, given the extent to which Orthodox Judaism clings to its old traditions, it should emphasize that it represents an “authentic self-contained system”, in contrast to other “watered-down” religions and denominations. Naturally this would be a “turn off” to a good humanist, but perhaps Orthodox Judaism shouldn’t be tailoring its message to humanists.

This brings me to my next objection:
Quote: “Theodicy has been a blunt tool for polemicists for ages and I think most people are uncomfortable with the idea that a man can know what God “intends” when disaster strikes. Pastors and rabbis are guilty of this and Prager is wrong when he says that the right wing elements of religions are better equipped to dealing with disaster than the more liberal sides. I don’t think either side is better equipped than the other. There are morons on both sides. Strident admonishments from televangelists about the wrath of God striking Haiti or Japan can’t be the solution that Prager is suggesting “works”. So I don’t quite know where he is going with this point.”

Every monotheistic religion is predicated upon a simple claim: Man *can* know the will of God. Without that claim, no one would claim to “practice” a religion, he would claim to know that God exists, a piece of information no more relevant to his life than the Czech word for watermelon. Moses, Jesus, Mohammed and Joseph Smith Jr. all claimed to know God’s will very well, and to see it not merely in his words but in his actions. This pushes many of their followers to make one very dubious claim, that God can violate the perceived rules of nature. But once they’ve made that claim, is it worthwhile to throw it out to avoid seeming like the televangelists? Consider that now they have to jump through another hoop, and claim that God has gone from being administrator of the universe (clearly reflecting almost all early religious beliefs) to merely its creator. They must reject all prayer for God help, or create convoluted explanations for how they serve a different goal than their contents imply.

Even once they’ve jumped through these hoops, religious revisionists still face a problem. They still need to learn the will of God to remain religious rather than simply Deists. Are they better off learning God’s will from a tsunami thousands of years ago or from a typo? The concept of an immanent God gives relevance to people’s lives and transforms religion from people talking towards a wall to real, personal interactions, with perceptible effect upon human lives. Humility may prompt a shift towards a less personal God, but I’m not convinced that it makes religion more appealing or, (presumably more importantly for the average religious person) that it solves any of the conundrums that religion must deal with in the first place.

(Note: This should in no way imply that picketing funerals, blaming someone for their loss, or any of the other garbage people engage in, is acceptable. Some measure of common sense, combined with recognition of the fairly universal rule in all moral systems that you don’t cause people unnecessary anguish (“al tehiye scumbag”), should be enough to prevent these kinds of actions. Providence doesn’t have to imply being a jerk.)

*There is quite a bit in Jewish tradition to argue to sides of this point, I’ve merely skimmed the surface. I merely wanted to respond to the latent assumption in the paragraph, that all forms of Islam must adhere to some established morality, and that Orthodox Judaism obviously does so.
