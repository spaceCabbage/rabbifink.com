Ignorance is Death

  • February 21, 2014
  • 25 comments
  • 0

Ramaz won’t invite a particular professor from Columbia University to speak on its grounds. Rashid Khalidi is a well known supporter of Palestinians and a former advisor to the PLO. His positions on Israel are diametrically opposed to the positions of Ramaz and more Orthodox Jews. Thus, it is reasonable that they do not give him the opportunity to speak on their campus. In general, Ramaz tries to provide the other side of the issues in Israel. It was deemed that Khalidi took this too far.

On Facebook I argued that he should be allowed to speak and Ramaz should provide a rebuttal to his position immediately. Teens are sophisticated enough to weigh arguments. They do it in Talmud study all the time. We are certain that our arguments will prevail, so let’s play it out and show our position is correct.

Rabbi Goldberg from Boca Raton Synagogue (along with several commenters) politely took issue with my position. Rabbi Goldberg’s basic argument is that there are positions that are too extreme and offensive to give a platform. He says that allowing Khalidi to speak is a pluralism that is too liberal invoking the famous “so open minded their brains fall out” expression to describe my thinking on this matter.

I appreciate Rabbi Goldberg’s thoughts on this matter because he does invite speakers from a wide range of political positions to his shul. I think that is amazing. But I think my angle on this is a little bit different. Interestingly enough, I saw something written 60 years ago that is illuminating on this subject and reflects my views as well. I think that his words are a fair response to those who took issue with my position.

A similar question was raised by Rabbi Lamm in 1954. The Cold War was heating up and Communism was a true threat to humanity. It was a given that Communism was harmful, hateful, vicious, and violent. There was a debate about whether it was appropriate to teach Communism in American public schools.

One school of thought held that teaching Communism would lend legitimacy to an evil empire. It was harmful to teach the ideals of Communism because it would be the first step toward American children becoming atheist KGB sympathizers.

The other school of thought held that all information is potentially harmful and we educate our children how to use it properly. So we teach chemistry even though it could give them the tools to build an atom bomb or cook meth. Our job is to teach responsibility and maturity and morality to ensure that our children make the right choices in life. (Also, the irony of controlling information in a democracy to fight totalitarianism was also raised.)

Rabbi Lamm felt that because this was a moral issue, religious leaders had the right to present their opinions on this issue.

His response was rooted in Chazal. The verse in Shoftim says to the Jewish people that when they reach the land of Israel they are warned “lo silmad la’asos k’toeivos ha’goyim ha’hem” – do not learn to do as they do in their abominable superstitions and immoral cults. Chazal say that the verse does not simply prohibit learning about the disgusting ways of the nations on Canaan. It does not say we cannot learn their history or scholarship. It says we cannot learn in order to engage in their practices. “Aval atah lahmed l’havin ul’horos” – you can learn to understand and to teach. We are not allowed to learn in order to practice in their ways, but we are allowed to learn in order to understand their ways and to judge their ways. After all, how are we to judge their ideas and their ways and their practices if we do not even know what they are? Of course there may be dangers, but there is danger in all knowledge.

Thus, Rabbi Lamm concludes that American public schools should teach Communism. Students should learn all about it and then they should be taught how to criticize Communism and understand why democracy is preferable. It’s a learning experience that is invaluable and based on the understanding of Chazal.

He rounds out the idea by saying that the Talmud is far more liberal in its publication and education of wrong ideas than we realize. We engage in explicit discussions about all sorts of lurid and immoral behavior while studying Talmud. We are not afraid that the student of the Talmud will take the information about the sins encountered in the Talmud and lead that kind of life. Sure, ignorance is safe, but so is prison and so is death. Life is fraught with risk and our job is not to limit information and knowledge, rather it is to learn as much as we can about our world and cultivate the tools to think critically about ideas that we reject.

Teach Communism. Teach why it’s wrong. That was Rabbi Lamm’s approach. This way of thinking resonates with me. I do believe there are limits to this idea, especially in the religious realm, but politically I think it’s certainly a good approach.

Of course it’s not perfectly analogous to inviting a supporter of the PLO who holds positions that if borne out would inevitably harm our brothers and sisters in Israel and the Diaspora. I prefer not to get into the specific details of what this particular speaker holds and whether he hates Jews. I happen to think his position is dangerous, if it would be carried out. I don’t think his ideas are going to become any sort of reality. I don’t even think there is a risk that his ideas will become reality. His words carry no actual effect. And so inviting him to speak for the purpose of rebutting his ideas is not only acceptable to me, I think it might even be preferable.

While I believe that this approach stands on its own, I know that Rabbi Lamm’s words carry far greater weight than anything I could ever hope to say on any subject. It was a complete accident that I saw his thoughts on teaching Communism last night. I share it with you because I think it’s relevant. Even if it’s not exactly on point, it paints a picture that is close enough to my view that I don’t think my position could be characterized as completely at odds with Rabbi Lamm’s position. In fact, I think it’s close enough that his words are instructive here.
