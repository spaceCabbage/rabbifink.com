Certainly many Orthodox Jews feel confident in doing mitzvot because they believe G-d commanded them just as many evangelical Christians feel confident asserting that Jesus saves because they have an emotional experience of Jesus that feels real to them. But when you get into the details of daily life, theism starts to look a lot more like humanism. Protestants change churches and practices all the time (ALL the time) because they feel “called” to do something else or because they reach a new understanding of G-d’s will by studying the Bible. Certainly, they believe that this is a message from G-d but from the outside, it looks a lot like making a decision about what’s best for you.
Similarly, while there is a lot of agreement in Orthodoxy about what G-d commanded, there is also a lot of disagreement when you get into something as common as what bracha to make over pizza or whether cholov stam is acceptable. In making those decisions, people are guided by family, hashkafa, study — all of which may change over time. The humanists and atheists I know spend just as much time thinking “what is the right thing to do in this situation?” as Orthodox Jews spend thinking “what does halacha say about this?” and evangelicals spend praying over the matter.
Those are all ways of trying to discern correct action. What they all have in common is the belief that it’s our job to figure out which action is most correct and then to take it.
Being human, we may fail. The atheist may listen to his baser instincts, the evangelical may decide that G-d wants him to do the thing he most wants to do and the Jew may decide that halacha magically aligns which what he thinks is right. There is equal potential for failure.
I think people who are committed to living a moral and ethical life has much more in common with each other than they do with people who do not care to examine their own behavior very carefully. I have more in common with atheists who believe in living an examined life than I do with fundamentalists who can’t explain why they have chosen to believe one interpretation of a verse over another.

Steve Savitsky interviewed Rabbi Moshe Benovitz( Savitsky Talks, “Technology and Social Media: How Are They Affecting the Post-High School Year in Israel?”, 8/1/12, 13:00 in mp3, some of which I transcribed below, and which is available on the OU website). He is not discussing proofs for God, but it is a related subject:

R. Benovitz:

…In the kiruv community, for example, they are coming to grips with the fact that some of the arguments– historical arguments, philosophical arguments– that like I said a charismatic educator could tell a person off the street and who would know better, is checked instantly on a hand held device that’s pulled out of a pocket. If those arguments do not hold water, then we’ve done more damage than good. We need to adjust to that, and we should adjust to that.

Steve Savitsky: Do you have an example of that?

R. Benovitz: …This is probably beyond the scope of this limited discussion because there are obviously complexities and layers here. But examples like mass revelation at Sinai being the only way possible, when you have challenges from other sources, the fact that Torah seems to have been forgotten in certain periods explicitly in the Navi and the like. The chain of the Mesorah– there is certain reason to believe that there were times where it was if not broken, but then it was down to a precious few; that’s a challenge, just to use one example, [to that] mass revelation argument of sorts. [Similarly there are challenges] in the scientific realm, and in the archaeological realm.

We need to be able to know that there is information at the fingertips of our students that of course we have answers to, and of course we have ways of responding to, but to just throw arguments out there, they’re not going to, nor should they simply accept at face value.”
