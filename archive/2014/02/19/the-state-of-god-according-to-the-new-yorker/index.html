The State of God According to The New Yorker

  • February 19, 2014
  • 13 comments
  • 0

In the New Yorker, Adam Gopnik, treats us to a broad, sweeping, insightful bird’s-eye of the fault lines between atheists and believers. The article is long and at times very dense. But it magnificently identifies and analyzes several incredibly important aspects of the trends in belief and disbelief.

The article, like most of this genre, focuses on the trends in America and American Christianity. I think some of it is entirely irrelevant to Orthodox Judaism, but much of it, and the bulk of Ross Douthat’s response in the NY Times feels ripped from the headlines of Orthodox Jewish discussions on the subject.

Gopnik analyzes several texts and recent books on belief and disbelief. It’s fairly thorough and a pretty impressive display of scholarship. There are three themes in the article that stand out to me. I will present my understanding of each theme, then the explanation of Gopnik’s point, and then how I think it relates to Orthodox Judaism in 2014 and beyond.

1. The lines between belief (the ayes) and disbelief (the noes) are blurrier than we think. More importantly, the differences are so minute and so subconscious that they almost hardly matter.

There are no true believers and there are no true rationalists. Everyone is kind of in the middle. Believers are seizing upon smaller and smaller gaps to demonstrate the need for a God. Non-believers, while rejecting the irrationality of belief in a deity are still ritualists who engage in fluffy behaviors that are not justified by pure evidentiary claims. In other words, believers are conceding a lot more than they used to concede and non-believers still believe in more than they care to admit. Humanism requires the belief in humanity. That’s just a new form of theism.

A lot of the article is a wishful thinking and projection. That doesn’t make it untrue, but it makes it feel less authoritative. This is an example of a claim that is somewhat true but not true enough. There are true believers. There are plenty of people who subscribe to the view of God that is sort of Heavenly Referee keeping score of everyone’s good and bad deeds. I know tons of people that think this way. In a sense, I think almost all religious Orthodox Jews, Christians, and Muslims believe this to an extent. It may be true that some abstract thinkers and writers and slowly rejecting this view, but it is still the prevailing religious view as far as I can tell.

However, there is a movement, an undercurrent, that is trying to normalize a different view of God. It’s a more rationalist view that indeed does take the smaller view of God. It’s not that God can’t do anything. It’s just that God doesn’t do all that stuff all the time. And that a lot of the religious legends are allegory, or metaphor, or idiomatic. The tiny voice of this view is barely audible. For some reason, Gopnik hears it louder than anyone else.

The argument that Humanism is a new form of theism is worth a second look too. While it’s true that humanism requires a belief in humanity, that’s a lot different than belief in God. It’s not just a small difference too. One is subjecting one’s will to an all-knowing God. The other is an appeal to popular morality. One is imposed. The other is chosen. Therein lies the supreme distinction between true religion and non-religion. As much as we may try to rationalize belief or ritual, at the end of the day, adherence to any theology and its attendant practices is an act of submission to something beyond us, while humanism is quite literally within us. No matter how small the God of Rationalist Judaism appears to be, God is still the driving force behind the entire enterprise.

2. The believers believe in a smaller God than believers from the days of yore. People don’t experience the supernatural anymore. But belief is increasingly mysterious and personal. It lacks the same conviction and certainty that the non-believers attribute to the believers.

Science has answered many of the questions that God used to answer. Technology has eliminated most fantastic legends of miracles and supernatural marvels. There is quite literally almost nothing in our world that cannot be attributed to natural phenomena and no act superseding the course of nature has ever been captured on a recording device. As God appears to shrink because of modern science and technology, belief takes on a new form. Belief is now being classified as a personal experience and described in terms of mystery and subjective sensations. We no longer talk of a God of miracles and salvation. We talk about a God that is found in the cracks and crevices of our personal lives.

Here Gopnik gets to the core of the issue that separates the two groups. What do we do about mystery? The believers attribute mystery to God. The skeptics shrug their shoulders and say they don’t what it is, but it’s not God. The old mysteries were too great, too insurmountable to avoid God. But now, the little mysteries that are left are not so compelling as to require us to believe in God. Both groups feel the tension. Both groups are aware of the mysteries of love, music, art, human frailty and wonder. Believers see God in that magical place and non-believers have no problem seeing nothing beyond the immediate landscape.

This is another very interesting point when we look at it through the Orthodox Jewish perspective. In general, Orthodox Judaism doesn’t bother with explaining or justifying belief in God. It’s a mitzvah to believe so we believe. Of course we believe. Only a corrupted person wouldn’t believe. So the question is avoided completely. When pressed, we are treated to familiar arguments from the Kuzari, the First Cause argument, the argument from Morality, and the various “proofs” from the text of the Torah itself. So the sort of belief being described here by Gopnik is pretty foreign to most Orthodox Jews.

But here again, there is a growing sense in our community that these proofs are insufficient at best and counter productive at worst. Instead, it is proposed that Judaism follow the path Gopnik describes. It is a form of belief that is muddy and uncertain. It is a belief that requires more conviction and confidence to possess, but has less conviction and less confidence of its own. Right now, this is an anathema in Orthodox Judaism. We are very certain of our beliefs and we vociferously proclaim our certainty. But it might be time for something with a little more finesse. It might be time to reevaluate the importance of certainty and whether we must insist on certainty. There is a trend towards a more mysterious and unexplainable version of belief. Will it catch on in Orthodox Judaism? It seems unlikely. [I have a more robust article on this specific subject in the works. So stay tuned.]

3. Belief will continue to wane until it is obsolete.

Gopnik concludes with a big bang. God was invented be miserable people with tiny little lives. We are rich. We are happy. We live large. We’ve outgrown God.

This is quite the presumption. Narrowly limiting God to lesser people would probably make God an endangered species. But I think this is far too narrow. The new version of God is not about solutions to life’s challenges and defeats. The new God is a God of mystery and wonder. We still marvel at sunsets. We still bask in the glow of love. We still feel our hearts soar when we listen to our music. We are not completely deterministic. We believe we can improve our lives and the lives of others. We have plenty of room for God in the personal struggles and triumphs of our admittedly fat cat lives. I see no reason that God would only appear as a response to pain and suffering. The mysteries of joy and pleasure and happiness demand, or maybe request, that God be present. Indeed, utopia is often described as the time and place where God is most present.

In Orthodox Judaism, there is a tendency to rely on the crutch of anti-Semitism or existential spiritual threats like intermarriage or the Internet to promote our religion. This supports the theory that God only works for the oppressed and besieged. This needs to change. We cannot define Judaism by what threatens Judaism. We cannot lie about our place in the world for the sake of creating feelings of desperation. The myth is too easily debunked. Judaism in America must be built on positive beliefs and appreciation for the values and rituals of Orthodox Judaism. We must create a space for God in the good moments and the triumphs. We must be willing to embrace a world that is wiling, even eager to embrace us. The kind of Orthodox Judaism that will thrive in friendly conditions is a different Orthodox Judaism that is being transplanted from the Shtetl to Main Street USA in 2014. It’s the wrong model. It’s full of agony and despair. That’s not our Judaism. That’s not our world. It is imperative that we adjust our focus and transport the worldview of contemporary Orthodox Judaism from the ghetto to our modern world.

In conclusion, I think there are three strong lessons for Orthodox Judaism present in the discussion of this article. First, God might be getting smaller, and religion might be getting more rational, but it will always be different than Humanism because its morality is not voluntarily imposed. Second, Godliness is found in the personal struggle and mysteries of life at least as much as God is found in certainty. And finally, Judaism needs to find a way to cheer up a bit and find a place for God in our triumphs and not rely on fear to make Judaism alive.

I strongly suggest reading the article in The New Yorker and the firm rebuttal in The New York Times. Let me know what you think in the comments.

  • ednastvincent

    I guess I don’t see the large difference between humanism and theism. Certainly many Orthodox Jews feel confident in doing mitzvot because they believe G-d commanded them just as many evangelical Christians feel confident asserting that Jesus saves because they have an emotional experience of Jesus that feels real to them. But when you get into the details of daily life, theism starts to look a lot more like humanism. Protestants change churches and practices all the time (ALL the time) because they feel “called” to do something else or because they reach a new understanding of G-d’s will by studying the Bible. Certainly, they believe that this is a message from G-d but from the outside, it looks a lot like making a decision about what’s best for you.
    Similarly, while there is a lot of agreement in Orthodoxy about what G-d commanded, there is also a lot of disagreement when you get into something as common as what bracha to make over pizza or whether cholov stam is acceptable. In making those decisions, people are guided by family, hashkafa, study — all of which may change over time. The humanists and atheists I know spend just as much time thinking “what is the right thing to do in this situation?” as Orthodox Jews spend thinking “what does halacha say about this?” and evangelicals spend praying over the matter.
    Those are all ways of trying to discern correct action. What they all have in common is the belief that it’s our job to figure out which action is most correct and then to take it.
    Being human, we may fail. The atheist may listen to his baser instincts, the evangelical may decide that G-d wants him to do the thing he most wants to do and the Jew may decide that halacha magically aligns which what he thinks is right. There is equal potential for failure.
    I think people who are committed to living a moral and ethical life has much more in common with each other than they do with people who do not care to examine their own behavior very carefully. I have more in common with atheists who believe in living an examined life than I do with fundamentalists who can’t explain why they have chosen to believe one interpretation of a verse over another.
