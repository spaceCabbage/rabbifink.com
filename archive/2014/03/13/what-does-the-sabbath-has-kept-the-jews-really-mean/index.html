What Does “The Sabbath has Kept the Jews” Really Mean?

  • March 13, 2014
  • 19 comments
  • 0

“More than the Jewish People have kept the Sabbath, the Sabbath has kept the Jews.” – Ahad Ha’am

I have heard this quote dozens of times as a sort of response to my recent article on Haaretz: A Jewish Lesson from the Malaysia Flight Tragedy

Some people care too much about the intent of the creator of art or the author of a book or essay. In their view, the only legitimate meaning of something is the intended meaning. Or maybe the other interpretations are valid but not as valid as the creators intent. Others say that once something has been given to the public, it becomes open to interpretation. No one person can impose their interpretation on another. Any idea that can legitimately be traced back to the source is a valid meaning. However, it cannot be said that this subjective meaning was the intent of the creator. It really just reflect the position of the interpreter.

One book that subject to great interpretation and metaphor is Huckleberry Finn by Mark Twain. High schools teach the book as a metaphor, college students write essays exploring its metaphors, and that there are lessons and meaning to the text is taken for granted. In the introduction to the book, Mark Twain writes:

Notice
PERSONS attempting to find a motive in this narrative will be prosecuted; persons attempting to find a moral in it will be banished; persons attempting to find a plot in it will be shot.
BY ORDER OF THE AUTHOR, Per G.G., Chief of Ordnance.

It hasn’t stopped anyone so far. The point is that people will find meaning in art and literature even against the protest of its creator. That’s okay. I think that once something is in the public domain, the public has a say in its meaning. I also think that it is possible that the creator’s feelings and beliefs will be subconsciously projected onto his or her art and literature. Thus, despite protestations to the contrary, meaning might actually be found in a text.

While I am a big proponent of seeing art as art and not limiting it to the precise meaning of its creator, I find the use of this quote in the context of God protecting Jews who keep the Sabbath from harm a tad ironic.

Ahad Ha’am believed in Cultural Zionism. In fact, he is credited with inventing it. This means that he was less interested in political autonomy and governing a Jewish Homeland than he was interested in preserving Judaism by reinforcing cultural and social aspects of Judaism. His feelings about Judaism were informed by the idea that our cultural and social connections are vital to our survival as a people. He saw people leaving Judaism and he thought that appreciating our cultural and social roots would imbue Jewish people with a sense of pride. Cultural Zionism was his big idea to save Judaism.

To Ahad Ha’am, survival as a people simply meant that we retain our identity as Jews and live proudly as Jews on a national level. When he said “Sabbath kept the Jews” he was arguing that non-Orthodox Jews should find value in recognizing Shabbos, even in a minimal way, so that we would retain Jewish identity. The Sabbath gave people an opportunity to reflect and connect one day per week. It also helped distinguish between Jews and non-Jews in a cultural sense. Ahad Ha’am believed that a strong sense of Jewish identity and pride is a direct result of observing some sort of communal Sabbath. He is right about that.

When he talks about how the Sabbath kept the Jews, he means it in a collective sense. It might be that each individual will have a Jewish identity because they observe the Sabbath, but that was not his focus. He was focused on the communal state of Judaism and in his view, the Sabbath was vital to our national survival.

I completely agree with Ahad Ha’am. Shabbos is a beautiful day that enables us to connect with God and our community once a week. It is perhaps the greatest innovation Judaism has brought to the world. It has indeed contributed greatly to our national pride and Jewish identity. Sabbath observance is a key to our future and the passing of our traditions from parent to child. Especially among the less observant Jews, Shabbos is an integral way to “keep the Jews.”

However, Ahad Ha’am certainly did not mean that keeping Shabbos will magically protect people who observe its rules. He did not think that God would save the Jewish people for keeping the Sabbath. He thought that the Jewish people would be saving themselves by keeping the Sabbath.

It is true that you can reinterpret the words to mean that if one keeps Shabbos, one will be protected from harm on an individual level, but he certainly did not mean that. So while I accept that it’s a valid interpretation, that would be the interpreters position, not the position of Ahad Ha’am. This is an important distinction. I’m also no sure that one can really fit this interpretation in with the words, but creative license is pretty broad.

People are free to say that Shabbos observance has spiritual powers that protect us from harm. I don’t think this is the proper way to view Shabbos, but I can live with it. People can even use this quote from Ahad Ha’am to make their point in a creative manner. But we cannot say that this is what Ahad Ha’am intended with his famous and exquisite quip about Shabbos.

Perhaps the resurgence of this quote will inspire us to spread the light of Shabbos to more people. Preferably, the light will be shining upon the beauty that is inherent to Shabbos and we will be able to protect ourselves from losing our Jewish identity by keeping the Sabbath.
