If the Rabbis Made Mistakes, Why Listen to Them?

  • March 20, 2014
  • 64 comments
  • 0

Several readers contacted me with an important question that was left unanswered in Life is in its Struggles: Dealing With the Tough Questions. The basic question is quite simple really.

If it is true that Chazal may have erred in matters of science or history or medicine, why should I listen to the laws of rabbis if they made mistakes?

I’ve been asked this question a lot. The sort of people who ask this question range from Haredi Jews to atheist Jews. Everyone asks this question. It is a question that cuts to the core of contemporary Judaism and requires our attention.

There is a flippant deflection that works in short comment threads or casual conversation. That is, everyone agrees that Chazal made some mistakes. We have conclusions in the Talmud that prove various rabbinic opinions wrong. We have examples of rabbinic thinking that is literally impossible to reconcile with reality. Almost every normal Orthodox Jew is forced to acknowledge that Chazal were not perfect or infallible. Thus, the question is not any more a question on my view than it is a question on every view.

But this is a dodge. It reflects the question back onto the asker without offering a shred of hope for an answer. This very important question deserves a good answer. I believe the best answer to this question is found in its assumptions. Let’s unpack the question and see what we can discover.

“If it is true that the rabbis may have erred in matters of science or history or medicine” is a completely fair way to set up the problem. I do express the view that Chazal may have erred in these matters. I have stated that for this reason it does not bother me when Chazal say things that contradict science or common sense. They were working with what they had and this was their best attempt. But today we know more than Chazal when it comes to the natural sciences. This is obvious and not troubling at all.

The next part of the question asks why we should listen to rabbis who made mistakes. In other words, if they were wrong about women or astronomy, maybe they were wrong about Jewish law too! In order to ask this question, a huge assumption is being made. Eliminating this assumption is the real answer to the question.

We are not required to listen to Chazal because of some sort of doctrine of infallibility. We do not follow the teachings and rulings of Chazal because the social reasons for their decisions are eternal and inflexible. Chazal do not derive their authority from the perfection of their logic or reasoning. The reason we adhere to the religious conclusions of Chazal is a matter of authority. Chazal have religious authority to set and determine religious law. There are limits to this authority. Chazal could not do whatever they dreamt up on a whim. Their authority was governed by specific rules and boundaries. But in the areas that Chazal did indeed possess authority, we are obligated to follow their rulings. We follow their rulings regardless of the social conditions and scientific understandings that may have influenced their opinions. Those are not relevant to their authority. Chazal had religious authority that was not dependent on explaining their rationale or providing sophisticated justifications. Their authority applied no matter the circumstances.

It’s similar to the American law that prohibits driving faster than posted speeds. The reason for those laws is ostensibly safety of drivers, passengers, and pedestrians. If scientists determined that it was actually no safer to drive 35 MPH in a posted residential zone than it would be to drive 42 MPH in the same zone, the law would still apply. You wouldn’t be able to drive 42 and argue in court that the science demonstrates that the law’s reasoning is incorrect and therefore it should no longer apply. It is completely irrelevant. We are all bound by the law because the law is the law no matter what. It is an issue of authority. The government has the authority to make laws. There may be some reasoning for the law, but the law applies even if the reasoning does not apply.

This is the difference between law and logic. If there is legal authority, the law is binding. If a law is illogical, it is still binding. But when arguing matters of debate and rhetoric, logic wins the day. If one’s position has no logical basis, one will lose the argument. Logic and law operate in different spheres.

Chazal’s authority is legal not logical. They have the authority to set religious laws and their logic is usually not relevant at all. If Chazal made a rule about women based on their possibly flawed understanding of women’s anatomy, the law would still apply. It’s a legal argument, not a logical argument.

Clearly a distinction could be made between areas that Chazal exempted women and areas Chazal made prohibitions. Also, when Chazal make a law and say it is because of X, then it may be permissible to undermine the law if X is disproved or incorrect in a particular instance.

The opening question is based on a flawed assumption. Some people think that Chazal had magical powers and were able to divine the will of God through these powers. If this is true, then everything they said would be expected to reflect God’s truth. Demonstrating any errors of logic or science in that kind of system undercuts the whole thing. The entire authority of the rabbis in that version of understanding is based upon infallibility. If it can be shown that they were not infallible, the authority is gone. This kind of thinking is far too common. It shows up on the extreme fundamentalist side of the religious spectrum as well as the extreme to the non-fundamentalist side. The question crosses typical lines, but the assumption that is required to ask this question may be incorrect.

There are two unanswered questions that are raised by this answer. First, where did Chazal get their authority? And second, what are the parameters and limitations of this authority?

Both of these are good, answerable questions. I have a lengthy essay that handles these questions and I hope to have that published somewhere, someday. In the meantime, it should be sufficient to make the basic distinction between legal authority and legal reasoning. The errors we find in Chazal are of no legal consequence and therefore we can be comfortable saying that Chazal made mistakes without undermining their legal and ritualistic teachings.

  • Dov Kramer

    Your main point (that they have the legal authority even if it was based on flawed information) is accurate, but insufficient nonetheless.

    Taking your example of driving 42mph in a 35mph zone, you are correct that the judge in traffic court will not allow the scientific safety study to affect whether the driver is found guilty, but I’m sure you’ll agree that the motivation to not drive 42 rests only on the fear of being caught, not the fact that it is against the law. There is no “traffic court” that decides the fate of one who breaks Jewish law, so even if one agrees that Chazal had the authority to decide Jewish law, there is no fear of being found guilty of breaking their perceived to be flawed laws.

    What about G-d, though? Will G-d punish someone who broke a flawed law? From a traditional perspective, yes, He will. Merely disobeying laws made by those to whom He gave the authority to make the law is enough to warrant punishment. I think you’d agree with that, but it wasn’t clearly stated in your piece. This was, IMO, just the first insufficient aspect; it needs to be clearly stated that because Chazal had the authority to determine what the law would be, we will be held accountable in the heavenly court for not obeying that law. And this is true whether the law was made based on misinformation or not.

    The second area where there is a deficiency (and this one I’m not sure you’d agree with) is about the role of divine guidance in the law-making process. Since Chazal had the authority to make the law, and the laws they made would impact G-d fearing Jews for hundreds, even thousands, of years, we can be confident that G-d would intervene in the law-making process so that flawed laws that would adversely impact religious Jews for generations are not made, or are at least mitigated (see page 2 of http://www.aishdas.org/ta/5765/shemini.pdf). If we are confident in G-d still being involved in the world He created, and confident that He gave Chazal the authority to determine the law, we should also be confident that the final result (the laws we must keep) are of real value to us (beyond just being obedient). This is true even if the reasoning Chazal used to make the law was flawed.

    The third area where your piece is deficient (and I think you may agree with the first part, but doubt you’ll agree with the consequences), is regarding the respect the sages are due. Even if they relied on the science of their time, they were still great and holy men, and their words and perspectives have immense value even if the paradigm they were working within had some misinformation. From this perspective, it is how they reacted based on the information they were working with that still has real value for us. For example, even if the medical advice they gave was primitive because of the state of medicine in their time, we can definitely see that they valued the scientific knowledge that was available to them, and acted accordingly. We may know that blood-letting has little medicinal value; knowing that they were in favor of regular blood-letting shouldn’t lead to the conclusion that we should do the same, but that we should follow the advice of the medical community in our times just as they followed the medical advice in their times.

    More-so, from a religious perspective, basing themselves on flawed science in scientific matters makes little difference when it comes to religious matters. Most of the lessons of the Talmud (and the corpus of Jewish literature that is based upon it) is not relevant to how accurate their science was. The very notion of equating a lack of scientific accuracy with a lack of religious accuracy is preposterous. The question you posed is “why should I listen to the rabbis if they made mistakes.” Aside from the fact that they had the authority to determine the law, we should listen to the rabbis because they were religious giants, and their mandates for living a religious life are extremely valid and valuable, and are no less so just because they weren’t all-knowing regarding scientific fields. No, we shouldn’t follow their medical advice, but we should follow their advice when they say go to the doctor and follow his or her instructions. And we should certainly follow their advice when it comes to living a life the way G-d wants/expects us to.
