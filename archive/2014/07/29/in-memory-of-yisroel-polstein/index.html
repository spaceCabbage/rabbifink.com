In Memory of Yisroel Polstein

  • July 29, 2014
  • 24 comments
  • 1

For two years of my life I had a best friend. We studied Torah together, we fought and argued, we got into trouble, we were best friends. He remains inseparable from my 9th and 10th grade experience.

At the end of 10th grade, my best friend began behaving more and more erratically. He could not stay in yeshiva. We spoke on the phone and he told me about learning that he was suffering from some mental health issues. He told me stories about life with mental illness. He was struggling. His life and mine parted ways simply because he was far away and we had little opportunity to connect.

Yis Polstein, Mark Paley, Eliyahu Fink (Purim 2014)

I never had a friend like that again. I had plenty of friends. But never a best friend like I had in 9th and 10th grade.

My friend was no longer part of my life, but I kept my ear to the ground. Whenever I saw people who knew him, I would ask about my friend. The answer was always the same. He was really struggling. Eventually he turned to drugs. First using, then abusing. Somehow he survived a few volatile years and I heard he found his way to Neve Tzion and then Bais Yisroel.

I visited Israel when I was 19 and went to Bais to see my old friend. We walked around Neve Yaakov for hours. We reminisced. We caught up. We laughed and learned again. My friend told me how he used Torah to beat his drug addiction and his mental illness. He was high on Torah and living life the right way. I was happy to hear it, but not confident that it was sustainable. I begged him to stay the course with his medications. To no avail.

Over the next 10 years I kept tabs on my friend. Some of the time he was doing magnificently. He was even invited by Torah Umesorah to speak at their convention about drug abuse. Life was good. But other times, he was struggling mightily. Drugs and homelessness drifting in and out of his life. He vacillated between small jobs and intense drug rehab.

We reconnected on Chanukah of 2012. That was my Chanukah miracle that year. He was living in LA and he heard I was the rabbi of The Shul on the Beach so he stopped in during our Chanukah party.

We talked for a while and promised to stay in touch. My long lost friend was very frank about his struggles. He had his 45 day chip but he knew he wasn’t done with rehab. He knew his destiny was to struggle with addiction.

Between more rehab stints, he joined us for services and events and dinners at the shul. He was connecting with his Judaism and making good friends. We took good care of him. He was loved by everyone and I loved having my friend back. He would beam with pride that he was my chavrusa (study partner) in 9th grade and that somehow I had “made it” where he could not. He felt like he was part of my moderate success, at least by comparison, and it was my pleasure to have my old friend along for the ride.

In March of 2014, my friend asked me to perform his wedding ceremony. He was clean, he was in love, and he was getting married. I was scared for him and for her, but he was getting married regardless of my fears. So I agreed to do the wedding. The wedding party was small, intimate, and one of the most beautiful experiences of my life, personal or professional. I think someone filmed it, and I hope that I will get to watch it some day. It was a kosher marriage because that’s what they wanted and we streamed it on Skype so his family could watch from afar. I spoke from my heart, I sang from my soul, and I cherished every moment of the experience.

Then tragedy struck again. My friend fell back into drugs. I helped coordinate the divorce. It was best for everyone. I was sad and I wanted to help my friend, but he was in too deep. I reached out to him several times. I didn’t hear from him ever again.

And now he is gone. Just like that. Snuffed out at the age of 32. Baruch Dayan Emet.

A lot of things make me cry. Death is not usually one of those things. But I’ve been crying intermittently for hours now. I can’t describe what I feel, but I know it hurts. My best friend from high school was never coming back, that much I’ve known for almost two decades. But I was accustomed to this new version of my friend and I loved him too. I didn’t think it was going to end. I knew it could, because addiction doesn’t go away, but I always knew that even when he fell, he always bounced back. Except this time he didn’t.

So now I say goodbye to Yisroel Polstein. I love you like a brother. I always will. Your strength and perseverance in the face of the most devilish of challenges was inspiring. You beat your inner demons so many times. Every single one of your victories was incredible. And you did it time and time again. The final tally will reflect the dozens of times you won. You only lost once.

You were one of the smartest people I ever met. It was a privilege to learn Torah with you. It was humbling too. You didn’t incorrectly answer a single question on a single Rabbi Cook exam. I don’t think anyone had ever done that before nor is it likely to ever happen again.

You were a great friend to so many people. I was just lucky to have been one of them. You were kind and thoughtful and fun to be around. That was the real you. Those other moods were the demons. They weren’t you.

I will miss both of the Yisroel Polsteins I knew. I will continue to miss my best friend from 9th and 10th grade who we called Yissy. And I now I will miss the adult Yis who impressed us so much with his magnanimity and might. It’s not just me. It’s the hundreds and hundreds of people who felt close to Yis who are mourning right now. We all miss Yis and we are all in pain right now.

For now, we mourn and remember our dear friend and our favorite tortured soul. Yis will be with us forever and we will be grateful for every moment we spent together. Each moment was a blessing.

We will try to do what you were so good at doing. We will try to bounce back. We will try to live life in the face of tragedy. Your strength will be our strength.

We will try to complete the great work you started while you were with us. We will help those in need. We will love selflessly. We will fill the void you leave.

Goodbye Yis. Rest in peace. We will fight your battles now.
