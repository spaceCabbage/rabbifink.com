I respect his erudition.
The last line of the article was “Let’s start the conversation.”, so I’m doing just that in an honest, open way. This is not a bashing session.

I have family members that suffer from “clinical depression”. I’m a fairly learned person in Torah and in physical sciences (I’ve been educated in Yeshivot, attend Daf Yomi and have a degree in Aerospace engineering).
The thing that really has me honestly perplexed and very disturbed is these two items:
1. There are very “heavy hitter” Torah commentators from hundreds of years ago (who were supposedly much more spiritually advanced than us) that claim ALL illness (mental and physical) are the result of averot (sin). Now, that doesn’t jive at all with this article. And that leaves an obvious problem for anyone honestly trying to resolve Torah and “science”.
2. Now, read this till the end before you judge: One of the family members I mentioned above leads me to believe that many cases of “diagnosed clinical depression” are false, much the way it is being discovered recently that as much as 30-40% of diagnoses of ADD/ADHD are false. This person was on various cocktails for years (from “professionals”). The cycle was: Got better for short time, then got worse than when she started on the cocktail. I “put my foot down” and “forced” her to go cold turkey and quit medication altogether and just continue counseling (no meds). Guess what: She just got better and better and she’s exercising, dieting, attending shiurim (this is a year later, so it’s not just a fad). Is she perfectly happy all the time – certainly not, but she’s light-years above where she was when she was being medicated into deeper depression (or oblivion). I’ll never forget the terminology used by the psychiatrists: “Well, it’s not working with X set of meds, so let’s try something else and see how it goes”. This is AFTER the same cycle for YEARS. Well, I figured, why not experiment with dropping the meds altogether. His guess seems no better than mine (this was not the only person to prescribe meds). And as I pointed out, what I guessed worked! Many of you can probably guess who “she” is.

I just want the parent/spouse/child of the depressed to know that the doctors are clueless in many cases. In direct contradiction to the author’s advice, I advise you to invest the painful/exhausting effort doing what you know is right for the depressed if you have a clue/insight. If not, try to find out – you know that person better than the doctor. Do NOT take the doctor’s advice as “irrefutable truth”.
NOTE: This does not mean I think there is no such thing as an identifiable chemical imbalance in the brain as a real disease, but “brain waves” are hardly conclusive evidence of anything at all. The brain waves just prove that depressed people emanate certain electromagnetic patterns. The CAUSE of the brain waves can be any one of a number of things. I’m sure some of you have read about certain high-stress situations and/or drugs causing brain waves that are for all intents and purposes identical to “clinically depressed people”. The scientific “evidence” and understanding is FAR from conclusive.

]]> By: Noam Greenberg http://finkorswim.com/2014/08/12/lets-talk-about-depression-and-mental-health/#comment-16916 Tue, 12 Aug 2014 14:43:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=8698#comment-16916 I’ve been thinking about this for a long time, and I even wrote a little bit about it years ago on my blog. The stigma against mental illness and against psycho-pharmacology is tragic.

But here’s something that occurred to me late last night. We should stop saying that a depressed person who commits suicide “killed himself.” Instead, let’s say he “died of depression.” Maybe it’s “just semantics” but sometimes a subtle shift in language helps. It will help people realize that depression can be, and all too often is, a fatal illness. The mechanism of how the the death occurred is, to me, less important than the actual cause of it.

I remember when Philip Seymour Hoffman died, a friend of his wrote that we shouldn’t say he died of an overdose. He died of heroin. To an addict, the death is a long series of events leading up to the time he dies. It’s the drug that kills him, the long-term addiction that makes escape so damn near impossible, not one time that calling it an overdose makes it seem like he just accidentally took too much. There was nothing accidental about it.

I think we need the same or something similar with mental illness. Whether it’s Robin Williams of one of thousands of others, the events that lead up to the actual death are just as important as the final choice to end it. It’s not a snap judgment or a mistake. It’s the result of a long fight with a fatal illness, and we need to start seeing it that way.
