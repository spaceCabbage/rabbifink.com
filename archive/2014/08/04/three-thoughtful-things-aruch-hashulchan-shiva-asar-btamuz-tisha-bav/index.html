Three Thoughtful Things in the Aruch HaShulchan on Shiva Asar B’Tamuz Through Tisha B’Av

  • August 4, 2014
  • 18 comments
  • 0

My go-to halachic source is the Aruch HaShulchan written by Rabbi Yechiel Michel Epstein. His son was Rabbi Baruch Epstein, the author of the Torah Temimah. I love both of their works. The Aruch HaShulchan is remarkable because it cites several opinions for many laws as it goes into a bit of detail for each section of law. It’s not just the law, rather it also include backgournd, dissenting opinions, and discussion. But my favorite part of the Aruch HaShulchan is where it goes off script and either preaches to its readers or provides fascinating social and historical data.

I try to review the relevant laws as we travel through the the yearly cycle to refresh my memory. Looking through the laws that apply from Shiva Asar B’Tamuz through Tisha B’Av, I noticed several very interesting things of note. Here I present three:

Not Eating Meat or Drinking Wine for 8 Days

The accepted Ashkenazi practice is not to eat meat or poultry from Rosh Chodesh Av through Tisha B’Av excluding Shabbat. The primary reason for this custom is that we don’t want to eat the kind of food that was consumed in the temple during the week we begin to mourn for its destruction. In modern society, this is considered quite arduous and grown men have been known to complain that they can’t handle not eating meat or poultry for so long. But apparently this is a longstanding custom.

It seems that people in 19th century Lithuania complained at least as much as we do. Additionally, people would artificially create Siyum scenarios to permit the eating of meat to lessen the terrible strain of meat deprivation. But the Aruch HaShulchan finds this abhorrent. He says that making a Siyum in this manner is “meguneh” – disgusting, see below. There were some people who just ignored the law and ate meat anyway. The Aruch HaShulchan calls these people out and asks rhetorically how they are not embarrassed and ashamed to violate this law for the sake of eating meat?!

But then he says something that surprised me. The Aruch HaShulchan says that Jews should stop complaining about not eating meat and drinking wine. It’s not such a big deal. Furthermore, the non-Jews (Catholics) refrain from eating meat and fish for weeks and if they can do it, we can do it! We are the Chosen People, stop kvetching so much!

Clearly, he is talking about Lent. I think it’s awesome that he knew about Lent. I think it’s even more awesome that he referenced it in his halacha book. And I find myself very skeptical that it would be acceptable to refer to Lent in a book of Jewish law, today

On Siyumim

In general, the Aruch HaShulchan prefers that we just not eat meat for 8 extra days.  One cannot eat meat at a Siyum unless he would have gone to the Siyum during the rest of the year. One cannot wait a few days to make a Siyum so that their Siyum is in the 9 days. But one may start learning a tractate simply in order to make a Siyum because that just helps increase Torah study.

But what should one do? Should one make the Siyum in the 9 days if one fortuitously completes a tractate in the 9 days?

The Aruch HaShulchan says that the custom in his city was actually to wait until after the 9 days to make the Siyum and forgo the culinary benefits of a 9 days Siyum. His reasoning is that the best way to celebrate a Siyum would be in a time on the calendar without any sadness or happiness restriction. So if you want to maximize your Siyum celebration, obviously you would wait until after the 9 days. So that’s what they did. What a beautiful perspective on Torah and a Torah lifestyle.

Litvaks in Streimels

The context is a bit complicated and completely irrelevant to the small point that I want to make here. All of us associate striemels with Hasidic Jews. The Aruch HaShulchan mentions that in his decidedly non-hasidic town, the people wore shtreimels. So first of all, clearly shtreimels were not a Hasidic garb, they were just a garb.

At some point, the Litvaks stopped wearing shtreimels. The Aruch HaShulchan explains that the government forbade them from wearing shtreimels so they started wearing different hats.

I have no idea if this is historically accurate. But regardless, this is an interesting nugget. I hope it can corroborated.

 

So there you have it. Three thoughtful things for the 9 Days.
