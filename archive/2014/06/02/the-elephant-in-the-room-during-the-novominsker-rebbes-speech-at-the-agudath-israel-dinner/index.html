The Elephant in the Room During the Novominsker Rebbe’s Speech at the Agudath Israel Dinner

Much has been made of the recent speech by Rabbi Yaakov Perlow, the Noviminsker Rebbe, at the Agudath Israel of America Dinner. In short, he said that Open Orthodoxy is heresy and it will be as inconsequential as Reform and Conservative Judaism will be, that is they will be invisible. He called on the Modern Orthodox community, a group that does not typically look to Agudath Israel for guidance, to reject Open Orthodoxy. The Forward called his remarks “stunning” due to their divisive nature. The NY Times was appalled that Mayer de Blasio sat through the comments and made no protest against the perceived negative implications of what was being said about liberal Jews.

The official position of the Agudah is to stand behind the Novominsker’s words. In an official statement, the Agudah “Frumsplained” the remarks to anyone who might have been offended by their harshness.

“Rabbi Perlow’s focus was on Reform and Conservative Judaism, not on Reform and Conservative Jews – on ideologies, not on people. Indeed, the main focus of his remarks was on a movement that calls itself Orthodox – “Open Orthodoxy” – yet has also crossed boundaries that have long established the parameters of normative Jewish practice and belief.

Rabbi Perlow was simply reiterating what Agudath Israel leaders have been stating since the founding of the organization nearly a century ago, and what painful demographic realities have made increasingly clear in recent years: that there is no Jewish future in ideological movements that have abandoned foundation principles of the Judaism of the ages.

Rabbi Perlow, and the community of Orthodox Jews who look to him as a leader, have nothing but love and concern for all Jews, regardless of their affiliation, regardless of how misled they may be by their religious leaders.

Precisely because of such love and concern, Agudath Israel believes that it is incumbent upon those who care about their fellow Jews and the Jewish future to call attention to the dangers of deviation from classical Judaism, to prevent what Rabbi Perlow referred to as Jews “fall[ing] into an abyss of intermarriage and assimilation.” Such clarion calls are heartfelt expressions of Ahavat Yisrael, love for fellow-Jews, not its opposite Heaven forbid!”

I am willing to take this at face value. I don’t question that this is the intention and meaning behind the exhortations to rise up against Open Orthodoxy and force the hand of the Modern Orthodox community to choose between Agudah and Open Orthodoxy. What I do question is its effectiveness and its broader implications.

First of all, the Right Wing Modern Orthodox rabbinic leadership and many respected lay people have already clearly staked out their position on Open Orthodoxy in general and in particular with regard to some of the more public issues that have been raised. Most of the audible voices have distanced themselves from Open Orthodoxy and would do so whether Agudath Israel made a public proclamation or not. Therefore I find it somewhat disingenuous that the Agudah is rallying the Modern Orthodox leadership to its cause. In many ways, the Agudah is bandwagoning a Modern Orthodox cause, not vice versa.

Second, I can accept that the purpose of Rabbi Perlow’s remarks and the subsequent statement were l’shem shamayim and without malice. That doesn’t change the fact that many people were hurt by what they heard the Novominsker Rebbe say. He may not have meant it, but what they heard was hate, division, triumphalism, and the abject dismissal of any version of Judaism that wasn’t up to the Agudath Israel standards. A clarification that confirms some of those feelings and just argues that there is no hate accomplishes nothing. The people who were hurt are just as hurt after the statement. It would have been nice if Agudah had issued an apology for hurting so many of their Jewish brothers and sisters with divisive comments that are more appropriate for religious debate or discussion than public communal condemnation. That would have at least validated the feelings of those who were hurt and might have been able to bring some level of mutual respect and understanding to the discussion. Agudath Israel chose not to take that route for the sake of being super clear that there is no hate, just love, and that love demands that they take a harsh stance.

But as I wondered when Modern Orthodox leaders staked out their uncompromising positions on Open Orthodoxy, I don’t really see the point of all this posturing. Open Orthodoxy won’t disappear because of a harsh statement or halachic argument by rabbis from more conservative segments of Orthodox Judaism. I’m not sure if this would have ever worked, but it certainly isn’t going to work in 2014. Modern society is a giant marketplace of ideas, not a small restaurant with a totalitarian soup nazi making all the decisions.

The marketplace of ideas is a consumer driven market. If consumers are satisfied with Agudath Israel, or as Agudah calls themselves, normative Judaism, consumers won’t leave. If others are attracted to it, they will join the group. Similarly, Modern Orthodox Judaism has to be attractive enough to the people who are born into it for it to have any continuity. People who prefer one over the other will be able to pretty seamlessly migrant from one to the next. Open Orthodoxy is a new movement. It seems clear, that in a consumer driven market, there is a soft spot in the market that is driving Open Orthodoxy. This is the elephant in the room.

Does anyone want to know why? Has anyone thought about the reasons that people are interested in Open Orthodoxy?

It can’t just be that they want to “uproot Torah” l’shma. It can’t just be that they are the Satan, sent to test “normative Judaism” with its “drastic reformations.” It can’t just be that they are all bitter or unhappy people who want to destroy Klal Yisrael.

So then what is it?

I’m not sure. We can’t really be sure. I have my theories. But the answer to that question, or even just asking that question, is where we should be putting our energies. Whenever people choose a product that is not your product, the responsible approach is to find out why your customer chose the new product. The real sugya here is what drives people to change their allegiances and why they choose Open Orthodoxy. Almost every single Yeshiva Chovevei Torah alum chose YCT over a different yeshiva. None were born Open Orthodox. What is the thing that pulls them toward Open Orthodoxy?

When you can ask that question honestly and search for an answer sincerely, you don’t need to be divisive. You don’t need to be triumphalist. You don’t need to be dismissive of others who do things differently. When you are engaging the market by promoting and adjusting your product, you don’t even want to expend energy making proclamations and statements regarding others. You want to get your product just right.

It’s time for Agudath Israel to ask this question and begin the conversation.
