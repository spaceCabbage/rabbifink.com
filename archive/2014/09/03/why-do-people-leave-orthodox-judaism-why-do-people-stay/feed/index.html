As someone who left observant conservative judaism (kosher home with two sets of dishes, regular synagogue attendance, some shomer shabbos practices but not full orthodox observance), I often wonder about my own motives, and the idea that they may have been primarily emotion-driven resonates with me. So I guess I “wanted” to leave and yet “wanted” to not leave completely. And I find that fascinating too – the Jewish fear of completely losing Jewish identity, the contradictory voices saying “I don’t want this” and yet “I have to pass it on to my children.”

The first is that we do what we want. Obviously when we take an action it’s the action we want to take – otherwise we wouldn’t be taking it. It might be tremendously painful, but for one reason or another, we want to act. It’s an important philosophical point (probably made by Spinoza) but very narrow in implications.

The other point is that information tends not to play a role in what we want. That’s obviously not universally true: I might love Snapple and drink it every day (and even use it in my analogies) but if one day I learn that my Snapple was contaminated with cyanide, I won’t drink the Snapple. That’s highly generalizable. On the other hand in some cases (smoking is a good example) we won’t act in accordance with our rational judgments, we’d prefer to ignore those judgments and keep smoking.

Vaccines by the way, illustrate a different point: It is tremendously hard to change deep seated belief. That could be relevant to your argument, but it doesn’t make the same point as the previous one. Here the subjects beliefs remain the same, despite evidence that ought to have changed them.

How does this impact religion? You’re right that religious people are likely to be tremendously attached to their religion, and it would take a lot to make them budge from it. (Conversely, religion – particularly Orthodoxy -has a lot of requirements and less attached people are unlikely to maintain them.) But there are also forces in the other direction. Cognitive dissonance is a big one. People want to walk the walk, if they believe something they want to act according to those beliefs and they will grow increasingly uncomfortable acting against them. (This from personal experience.) And, especially over time, this *can* overcome existing attachments. It is possible to want two different things and eventually one side wins.

Is the number of people who leave Orthodoxy due largely to belief significant compared to the attrition due to other factors? That I don’t know. But it’s worth considering that these might be the types of people a religion needs. Good leadership requires both devotion and the ability to change – that is, unusual susceptibility to changing beliefs and acting accordingly. Which might suggest that the effects of intellectual attrition are far greater than the simple numbers might suggest.
