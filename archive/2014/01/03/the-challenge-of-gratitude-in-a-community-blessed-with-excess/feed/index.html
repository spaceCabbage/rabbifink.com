]]> By: Orthonomics http://finkorswim.com/2014/01/03/the-challenge-of-gratitude-in-a-community-blessed-with-excess/#comment-15116 Mon, 06 Jan 2014 13:24:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=7980#comment-15116 I’m not commenting on my own children since they have never attended a project-heavy school! My comments stem from years of observation of schools as well as conversations with parents regarding their children’s classroom experiences. I’ve also pinch hit for friends who needed a sub in a crunch and spent my morning 1) assembling projects for over an hour as the children were not given the time to assemble their own projects nor did the have the capability to do so at least within the environment and 2) shuffling children from one activity to another with nary more than 15 minutes on any one project. I remember sitting the 4 year olds down to paint and little more than 10 minutes had passed and they were told to clean up and move to the next part of the day (guess who cleaned up? Clue: not the children). I believe that this type of rushed environment is counterproductive educationally and that it does not actually benefit the child. For children that are not quite “in the box” because they are anxiety-prone, prefer a slower paced environment, or have a hard time switching gears, can and do go into full meltdown mode.

One of the goals of education, even or perhaps especially for younger children, should be to lengthen the attention span, to allow a child to really get into their work, to allow full enjoyment of a project. Another worthy goal is for children to see a project through from start to finish and when the teachers and assistants follow behind the children with a sponge, I do believe they miss out on an important aspect of their education.

I also attended public school and clearly remember than each teacher made their own copies and had a strict limit as to the number of copies that they could make for “free.” Contrast this to some yeshiva schools that I know of that actually have a full time employee for the copy room.

]]> By: MarkSoFla http://finkorswim.com/2014/01/03/the-challenge-of-gratitude-in-a-community-blessed-with-excess/#comment-15098 Fri, 03 Jan 2014 18:19:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=7980#comment-15098 Not only is yeshiva day school the largest chunk, but it dwarfs all the other expenses by FAR. I’ll explain two (and a half) reasons why this is so:
1. Numerically, even for a single child, but especially for the 3.5 average, it is much larger than all the other expenses combined (and I’m including the extra costs of housing in a frum area as well).
2. Most of the other expenses are optional (lechter aren’t required, can use tea lights on aluminum foil, etc). Yeshiva day school is not optional, it is required to be a fully functioning member of a frum community.
3. And for all those other expenses, there are wide variations in cost. Back to lechter, you can buy cheap $50 ones, or expensive $5000 ones. Or meat, you can buy chicken at $2/lb or meat at $15/lb. Or Pesach, you can go elaborate and spend thousands on extra fancy food ad decorations, or you can spend $50 for a few boxes of matzot plus another $120 for the 4 extra meals over a normal week with shabbat. For housing, you can buy a small 3 bedroom home or you can buy a 6 bedroom palace. And so on. When talking about tuition, there isn’t a wide variation in cost, maybe there are two or three schools in the area, but their tuition and fees won’t vary by more than a thousand or two max.
