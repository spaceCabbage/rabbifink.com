Equity feminists (feminism’s first wave) are principally concerned with equal opportunity in work and education. Gender feminists (contemporary feminism) are concerned with promoting the idea that men and woman are basically the same (they see gender as a social construct). The current debate about feminism is concerned with the second ideology.

You can see this distinction in the world of Orthodox Judaism. Expanding woman’s participation in prayer and ritual is problematic for two reasons. 1) There is little legal precedent (unlike woman’s education) and 2) Prayer, more than learning, is seen as the more basic expression of our fundamental nature. It will be different for men and woman. Contemporary critics of feminism are concerned with gender feminism which sees in every social inequality the evidence of sexism and discrimination. Why should combating this idea should be perceived as a threat to Orthodoxy’s future?

Here’s another question for you. Assuming you believe that gender is not the consequence of social conditioning, where do you think this difference is appropriately reflected in Jewish life?

And ideal society would be governed by halacha.
In that society, women would not be judges or lawyers and they would have no input at all over the laws that controlled their lives, except possibly as petitioners. Women would not serve in government, lest they hold authority over men. Women might not learn to read (it was radical in the 19th century). Women might not drive (some Chassidic sects disallow it). They would not learn in the same schools with men nor would men and women interact socially or professionally. Specific modesty codes could easily be added to the legal code.
This is halachic society — not the mixed-bag we live with where the general norms of life are feminist and halacha is quaintly confined to the Orthodox synagogue. Gender inequality and gender separation is deeply rooted in halacha. The black-hats in Israel are not making things up. Can you seek a way to permit such and such? Of course you can. May things can be permitted, if you need a reason to permit them. But in an *ideal* society, why would you do so? Large numbers of Charedi Israelis are asking themselves that question.
I don’t want to throw a damper on the MO march of progress, which I very much cheer from the sidelines. I admire the way MO Jews are slowly and thoughtfully engaging these issues. But to me, worrying about tefillin is a sign of not taking halacha very seriously. Because halacha is not a system for running a synagogue service. It is a system for running a society. Those who take halacha seriously should worry about bigger issues, like whether an ideal halachic society would look more like our lives here or more like Saudi Arabia and Iran.
Most MOs have no desire to live in Iran in terms of gender issues but instead of facing down all the ways that 12th and 16th and even 19th century legal codes actually envision exactly that society, they’ve made a deal. Secular society will provide them with equality, personal freedom, etc. and halacha will remain pure, if confined to the walls of the Orthodox synagogue. I don’t have the answer; I do admire you for engaging the challenge.
