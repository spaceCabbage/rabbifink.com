I am not permitted to disclose particular details, but I was privy to the court transcript of the end of the trial and court decision in the custody battle from yesterday’s post. My general thoughts have not changed. In general, I think that many women are scared that if they leave the ultra-orthodox community they will be forced to forgo custody of their children. This is a legitimate fear. That was my point yesterday.

Today, I will address this case and I will attempt to be brief.

The case is complex and there is no simple solution. Both of the parents are flawed human beings, just like the rest of us. Both parents have some merit to their case. The judge seemed to skew toward the father whenever it was a judgment call and away from the mother when it was a judgment call. The primary issue in the case, as always, is what is the best interest of the child.

One aspect of that was where the children would have less “confusion”. With very little support and with a dismissive wave of the hand to the mother’s expert, the judge ruled that having the children live a new secular life with their mother would be more harmful than continuing the same life with their father.

I think this was the weakest part of the case.

The children will still be seeing their mother on a fairly regular basis. Although she is required to adhere to the rules of the children’s’ school, they will know she is not chasidic like their father is. So it’s still confusing. I don’t see how this solves anything.

In other words, the court established as a fact that there would be confusion is she were awarded custody – this was speculative and rebuttal evidence was not really considered, then the court established that to minimize confusion the father should be awarded custody – this doesn’t seem to solve the problem. This was the extent to which religious values and practices were applied in the case. It was purely with regard to “confusion”.

In terms of this case, I think the evidence to support custody on either side was weak. Neither side made a great case. The “confusion” section of the decision was particularly speculative (which is ironic considering how the judge dismissed some evidence in favor of the mother because it was too speculative). In the trial transcript the judge clearly said that the mother has the right to choose to leave orthodox Judaism, however she cannot make that choice for her children. I found that troubling. I wonder how old or how young a child must be for a parent to have the right to take their child out of orthodox Judaism? Maybe never?

In terms of the general issues this case raises, I think there are some legitimate concerns. First, like I said above, I think that losing child custody is a fear that many women have when they consider leaving orthodox Judaism. I am not sure this is the best tactic for keeping people in the fold nor do I think it is a positive message from the Frum community. But I think this case opens the door to fighting this presumption. The court relied heavily on the “confusion” for the children if their mother was no longer ultra-orthodox. This can be rebutted. It might take some time, it might take some better experts, but by no means is this a fait accompli.Â Further, the court seemed to have been much more amenable to joint custody solutions if the mother would have committed to even the slightest observance. There is room in the most liberal sects of modern orthodoxy for very relaxed observance. It seems that this would have been enough to allay fears of confusion. This just goes to show how little sense the “confusion” issue makes.

In sum, the case was decided on several issues. It was a harsh decision that I don’t think solves the issues it purports to solve. It was not a purely religious decision. It seems that this case need not be a case that will impact future cases of women in similar situations if the chips fall just a little bit differently.

I empathize with the mother in this case, but I do not feel that the sole issue in the case was religious observance. It may have played to prominent a role, but I have a feeling she may have lost custody even if “confusion” was not at issue here. In the end, it is extremely painful to read, let alone experience, a judge deciding the fate of a family. I can only imagine what kind of pain the entire family is experiencing. In particular, the mother, who lost custody must be completely beside herself. I wish her much strength in putting the pieces back together and finding happiness and success in the next phase of her life.

A landscape shifting case was recently decided in Oregon. The court decided that the prosecution has the burden of proof to show that eyewitness testimony is reliable. This means that when a witness testifies, the jury does not need to assume the testimony is accurate. Rather, the prosecution must show that there were no factors that would make the testimony suspect.

The court based its reasoning on significant scientific evidence that has shown that we don’t always remember things as well as we think. We are very susceptible to the power of suggestion, especially from authority. Also, witnessing a crime can be a very traumatic event and our eyes and minds don’t function perfectly under those conditions.

The most confounding thing about misidentification is that our eyes and mind are so good at convincing us that we saw that we saw. Even when we are presented with evidence to the contrary we trust our own unreliable sight and memory over better evidence. Juries are no different. They also place too much weight on eye-witness testimony. It is almost like a Holy Grail of evidence but in reality it is extremely unreliable.

I worked on a case where an “eyewitness” testified that he “saw” the defendant running for his life (he had just allegedly shot someone) from 100 yards away across a very busy street fromÂ inside a cafe!Â Unless this guy had Superman’s Supervision, it would be impossible for him to see and identify the person in question. Yet, the jury was convinced that he was a reliable witness and perhaps more incredibly,Â he was convincedÂ that he was being truthful.

Under the new rules in Oregon, the jury would be instructed to be skeptical of such testimony unless they were convinced he was an accurate witness.

The meta significance of this ruling is that the courts were willing to discard their “tradition” in favor of science. Tradition has always told us to trust our eyes and trust the eyes of others. If we see something we can be sure that it’s true. But the science says otherwise. The science says that our eyes play tricks on us. Our memories are fuzzy and vulnerable to suggestion. In short, despite what we’ve thought for so long, our eyes do deceive us. For the courts to place a stake in the ground and reverse the conventional thinking in favor of new science is worth acknowledging.

I think this is hinted at in Jewish law as well. We have an obligation to judge people favorably. The Talmud says that if you “see someone who has sinned” you should not judge them unfavorably as well. Granted, other reasons have been supplied to explain this rule. But I think it fits in well with what we now know about “what we see”. That is, we cannot be sure what we have seen is real or true and thus it seems inappropriate to judge others simply based on what we have seen.

The 9th Circuit Court of Appeals issued a ruling that effectively allows the government to wiretap anyone they choose without fear of repercussion. In its ruling, the court stated that while a warrant-less wiretap was not valid, the victims of the wiretap could not sue for damages because of sovereign immunity (protection from suit for the government). This reversed a ruling in the District Court that awarded damages to two victims of an illegal wiretap.

The plaintiffs in the case were suspected of involvement with Al Qaeda. The government surveilled them without obtaining a warrant. They found out about it in the NY Times and sued. As it stands now, the government cannot be sued for this breach of Constitutional law.

It seems that the primary issue in this case on appeal was whether the language of the statute that prohibits unlawful surveillance constituted an explicit waiver of sovereign immunity. In its ruling, the Court of Appeals held that it did not. As support for their ruling, the court added that the legislature rejected a section in the law that would have waived sovereign immunity thus proving that it was left out intentionally.

Plaintiffs could sue for damages if the information is used. However they cannot sue for damages simplyÂ because the information was collected.

So we are left with a law with very few teeth. While the government is not permitted to spy on us, the government will suffer no harm for doing so unless it uses the unlawfully obtained information. This is easy to avoid, because once the wiretap provides enough information, the government can discover new information lawfully and rely on that new information for prosecutorial purposes.

The plaintiffs were spied upon under a Bush administration spy program. It seems we are now stuck with this law and its arcane ramifications.

It should not concern us that the victims of this illegal wiretap were Al Qaeda suspects. They were American citizens just like the rest of us. Any one of us can be a victim of similar illegal government action.

If Congress feels that the law yields a bad result they can amend the legislation to waive sovereign immunity. As citizens, we can petition our representatives in D.C. to do that. They don’t have to listen, but it looks like that is our best shot at protected ourselves from being spied upon by the government.

It is not the court’s job to fix the law. That is the job of the legislature. Let’s hope they fix it soon.

Rubashkin was convicted of 86 counts of bank, wire, mail fraud making false statements to a bank, money laundering and violations of an order from the Secretary of Agriculture. His sentence was 324 months (27 years) which was on the low side of the guidelines.

In 1999 Agri opened a line of credit with First Bank Business Capital. To secure the loan, Agri offered its inventory and accounts receivable as collateral. Meaning, the bank gave the loan assuming that Agri was making a certain amount of money. Agri inflated its accounts receivable in two ways. First, they fabricated invoices to increase the accounts receivable. These were not errors. They were deliberate. Some of the false invoices were to accounts that prepaid Agri, so no invoices were needed. Others were to accounts that sold no meat like a clothing store. Others reflected balances on accounts paid in full. Others just inflated the amounts owed. The court estimates that these false invoices fraudulently increased Agri’s credit line by $10 million.

The second way Agri inflated it’s account receivables was by depositing money that was supposed to be paid to the receivable account into a separate account. This gave the bank the impression that the receivable account was still owed more money than it was actually owed.

These acts were the basis for the money laundering charges.

Because I am not a “fan” of zoos or animal exhibits, I continue to wonder “what’s the point?”. And that is what I am going to write about here.

Animals belong in the wild. You want to go see animals? Go visit them in their home town. Don’t bring them to my home town and cage them up so people can gawk at them. Sure, there is educational value in learning about animals, but is that sufficient cause to lock animals up in a place that somewhat resembles their natural habitat?

I know, I know, we need to learn about animals so that we can understand our planet and how we relate to the ecosystem and we need to protect animals that are losing their natural habitats etc. etc. etc…

We can do that all in other ways. We have the resources to study animals in their environment.

Zoos hearken back to a time when Man thought Himself invincible and the world to be at His mercy. I think we know better know. Man is part of an intricate universe. We can’t overstep our bounds.

Who in their right mind thinks that an orca that is accustomed to swimming miles on end day after day is comfortable in a big fish tank? It is a miracle they haven’t revolted yet! I feel terrible for the trainer. Not just because she was killed by a killer whale, but because she probably thought that the animal was her friend. She probably had developed feelings for the animal. Most trainers do. I have heard from many trainers that they “love” their animals. But the thing is, animals don’t love like people do. Animals don’t reason or use logic. They can be trained to suppress their instincts, but they can always revert to their natural behaviors. I feel bad for the trainer because she probably thought the killer whale would never harm her.

Maybe I just don’t enjoy seeing animals in a zoo enough to see the value, but it seems hard to justify in my eyes. The danger to humans is clear and I believe that animals belong in the wild.

In Jewish law, an animal does not have “rights”. However, a human has responsibilities toward animals. We are not permitted to abuse animals. We are not permitted to cause animals any pain or suffering. We are only permitted to hunt for food (by most authorities) and not for sport. In my view, zoos are sport. Caging animals so that we can have fun seems like an abuse of animals. And we have a responsibility to treat animals with respect and care.

I would much prefer to watch the Planet Earth series (which you should DEFINITELY buy, click here) at home, than see animals caged in a zoo.

Just think, another life would have been saved too.
