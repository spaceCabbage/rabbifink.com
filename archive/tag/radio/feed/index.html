]]> Presenting another episode of Finkorswim Live on the Stunt Show. On this episode we got to some pretty substantial discussion of the current state and future of Orthodox Judaism.

The show aired at 1 PM ET on July 2, 2015 on NachumSegal.com and the NSN App. The show has been archived and is available as a podcast on this page.

I have been working on crafting a version of Orthodox Judaism that is uniquely American / Western Democratic and native to the 21st century. We talked a bit about what this means and why it’s necessary. Then we were joined by my superstar guest, Rabbi Doniel Katz.

In my view, Rabbi Katz is leading the charge in addressing these issues. We come from very different perspectives but our ideas overlap significantly. It was a great discussion and we could have done another 8 hours, at least.

]]> Presenting another episode of Finkorswim Live on the Stunt Show. Today we brought another discussion and conversation that is dominating the religious conversation in America to these radio waves.

The show aired at 1 PM ET today on NachumSegal.com and the NSN App. The show has been archived and is available as a podcast on this page.

Today we talked about the recent Pew Research Center study that demonstrates the decline in religion in America. I went through some of the proposed ideas that I think are off base and then we welcomed our guests to talk about what might work.

My guests were JR Foresteros, who is working through these issues with his Nazarene Christian congregation, and Rabbi Daniel Korobkin, has been somewhat vocally challenging the status quo in the way we approach Millennials and Judaism. Tune in. It was a lot of fun, and very fascinating.

]]> My childhood commute to school was pretty short in time, but long on vivid memories. My father drove my brother and I to school every morning for minyan at 7:15 AM from 5th to 8th grade. It was convenient because we went to school a the same place where my father worked.

The soundtrack was almost always the same. JM in the AM with Nachum Segal accompanied us for the 5-7 minute drive. Nachum Segal was part of my childhood, as were some of his favorite tunes from Safam and the HAFTR choir, to name a couple that always stuck out in my mind as quirky and less mainstream than MBD and Carlebach. My father drove us to school, but Nachum Segal got us there.

Today I met the man who retains such a lovely place in my memory and it was a blast.

I spent an hour at WFMU observing Nachum do his thing for a little while. Eventually we were live on the air and I was schmoozing with Nachum like old friends. We were both pretty surprised that 45 minutes had elapsed when all was said and done. It felt like we could have talked for 45 hours. The pretext for the interview was the previous post (Three Empty Chairs Can Fill Empty Hearts) but we barely grazed the surface of that topic and managed to discuss many other things.

Also, Nachum announced that I will be joining the Nachum Segal Network as a rotating host of The Stunt Show in the 5775 radio season. I am honored to be associated with the Nachum Segal Network and I look forward to bringing what we do here on the Internet to the radio waves. I am grateful to everyone at the Nachum Segal Network for bringing me into the fold and thank Nachum for today’s interview and this exciting opportunity.

Listen to the interview here: Nachum Segal Network

]]> I was invited to be a guest on Aaron Moeller’s weekly radio show on WRCR 1300. The topic was this: Great Idea, Terrible Execution.

My shul is in a place that might be more diverse than anywhere else in the world. My worldview is the complete opposite from the worldview of insular chasidic Jews in Monsey. So perhaps the idea was to use my experience in Venice to brainstorm ways to nudge the insular community towards a bit more tolerance and integration.

We were joined by Reverend Weldon McWilliams IV who has become the face of the Wake Up Rockland activist group. He is a wonderful fellow and I enjoyed our time together.

It was an interesting conversation and does dovetail into a passion of mine. Helping people and communities understand one another is very important to me and I hope that our conversation today can be the start of a journey toward more unity in Monsey.
