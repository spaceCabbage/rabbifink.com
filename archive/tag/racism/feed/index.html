I don’t know when or exactly how or why, but at some point in my childhood I became enamored with Martin Luther King Jr. and the speech he gave 50 years ago today. I remember that one of the first things I did on the Internet was listen to the speech. I will never forget the euphoric feeling I experienced when I heard Dr. King say “…when we allow freedom to ring, when we let it ring from every village and every hamlet, from every state and every city, we will be able to speed up that day when all of God’s children, black men and white men, Jews and Gentiles, Protestants and Catholics, will be able to join hands and sing in the words of the old Negro spiritual, “Free at last! free at last! thank God Almighty, we are free at last!”

The speech influenced me greatly and I had a faux archive version copy of the speech in my childhood desk drawer.

Ever since then I have shared the same dream as Dr. King.

“I have a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character.”

Content of their character. We are getting there. It’s very hard to avoid our natural biases. It’s a lifetime effort. It’s something everyone needs to work on and Dr. King calls upon us to do just that.

Similarly, I urge all of us to judge ideas by the content and substance of the idea and not the value we place in the person making the statement.

But I have a dream that in the Jewish community we can judge one another by the content of our character and not by our affiliations and style of clothing. My dream is that we can create a global Jewish community united by far more than the things that separate us.

One day, all Jews, no matter their belief in God, Torah M’Sinai, or the Afterlife will work together to provide positive Jewish experiences for our communities. One day, whether we are Zionist or indifferent, liberal or conservative,  we can form a united Jewish nation. One day, mystics and rationalists, GRAniks and chasidim will dance together. One day it won’t matter who one’s grandparents were or which town one’s family came from in Europe or “God forbid,” if they came from one of the Arab countries.

Dr. King’s dream resonates with all of us. It gives us hope for a better future. A future where pettiness and narcissism give way to tolerance and empathy. That is my dream.

How do we get there? We fight against drawing imaginary lines that only serve to divide us. We argue for the rights of those who are under-represented within our ranks. We strive to give our children every opportunity to succeed and find meaningful, joyous, passionate Judaism that is inclusive, understanding, and loving. We reach out to our ideological opponents and offer them friendship with no strings attached. We explore innovative and comprehensive solutions to our social problems instead of applying bandaids to severed limbs.

Yes, it is a dream. But it’s a dream I believe in strongly. It is a dream worth fighting for. It is a dream that Dr. King inspired in a little boy who grew up to be me.

Fifty years later, the speech echoes through my brain. It’s message cuts through the noise and sings a beautiful song of hope. Thank you Dr. King for inspiring generations of people and thank you for inspiring me with the greatest speech I have ever had the privilege to hear. On its 50th anniversary we say thank you.

I hope we will live to see your dream come true and all of our dreams as well.

Cross-posted at Medium

In the old days, when it was still cool to be a racist, people felt superior to other people because of the color of their skin. A white person was automatically better, smarter, more worthy than a non-white person and the average white person thought this was acceptable. This resulted in slavery, segregation, vigilante justice, and the dehumanization of black people.

Luckily, we have progressed as a society to the point where that kind of thinking is abhorrent and is considered wrong. That’s the good news.

However, we have also spawned a new form of racism. This is a much more subtle racism but equally nefarious.

In the wake of the George Zimmerman trial we have seen strong reactions from some members of the black community decrying the lack of justice for Trayvon Martin. There is an equally strong counter-reaction explaining that it’s okay to suspect black people of crimes because the statistics support suspicion. This latter group are the modern racists.

The statistics show that black people commit a higher proportion of crimes than white people. Leaving aside the difficulties presented by the bias of selective enforcement and selective prosecution, I argue that using these statistics at all is an example of modern racism.A statistic is only meaningful if the data being used is a factor in the conclusion. For example, (and this is completely fictional) if I were to show that the majority of red haired women possesed below average intelligence, that statistic would not be meaningful unless I showed a causation between hair color and inferior intelligence. If I can’t show that, the statistic is meaningless. Even if the statistic is “true” it still doesn’t tell me anything about other red haired women. It just means that red haired people are not a significant group with enough specific traits to be classsified as a group with particular characteristics and predictable tendencies.

Categorizing all black people as one large group where the acts of one black person in Florida somehow tell us something about a different black person in Seattle is racist. Even within the same community it can be offensive to group people together because they share a skin tone. If the only thing two people share is skin tone then we cannot make any meaningful inferences from one person’s behavior to the other person’s behavior.

If we make judgments for the entire arbitrarily created group, we are being racists. There is no reason that the act of a black man in Texas should mean more about the potential acts of a black man in Michigan than his acts should inform us about a a white Norwegian woman or an indigenous Korean man. The only reason to connect the imaginary dots between all people of color is because there is a fallacious assumption that all people of color share some attribute that makes what one black person does meaningful in the analysis of what another will do. This is racist.

It’s not racist in the old fashioned lynch mob way. But it is still racist to presume one knows things about one black person simply because another black person or group of black people act a certain way.

I find that this is a very difficult concept for some people. These people clearly think that there actually is a black predisposition toward illegal or dangerous activity.

We must not tolerate that kind of thinking in 2013.

I’m looking at you Pat Buchanan.

Why do we focus so much on anti-Semitism? A recent article on Aish.com makes a decent point that the State of Israel did not solve anti-Semitism. Herzl hoped it would end Jew hatred. Clearly it did not.

But for some reason the writer, Rabbi Blech, emphasizes that hatred of Jews is unreasonable and defies logic. Well obviously. Hating anyone for no reason is by definition nonsensical. The odd implication seems to be that hatred of others makes sense and hatred of Jews does not.

I disagree. There is irrational hatred all over the world. Is it rational to hate people because of the color of skin? Is it rational to hate any of the persecuted minorities and ethnic groups? Of course not.

Yet around the world and over the course of history people have been persecuted and hated for being just slightly different. Whether they are different looking, different speaking, different thinking, different anything. There are plenty of excused people give to hate. None of them make a bit of sense. Hate is universal. Us Jewish people have not cornered the market on being hated.

I think what makes anti-Semitism unique is that Jews have lived all over the world and been a minority in so many places that we have managed to have more people in more places hate us for no reason than anyone else. Other hated people are more localized. Seems to me, that is a more logical explanation than to create a qualitative distinction between hating Jews and hating everyone else.

Rabbi Blech says the true answer to anti-Semitism is that “we persist in fulfilling our mission to serve as the world’s conscience until all of mankind learns to acknowledge the beautiful truths of our heritage.” I don’t think this is a complete answer. Hatred of Jews defies logic. Being the world’s conscience won’t fix stupid.

The more complete antidote to anti-Semitism and all hate is to preach love and tolerance, to find common ground with others, to create opportunities for people to learn more about one another and realize on their own that there is nothing rational about hating people for the simple fact that they are different.

There is no rule that states that people must hate one another in order for the world to spin on its axis. It can end and it will end. But let’s not limit ourselves to working on Jew hatred. All hate is the same and all hate can be fixed pretty easily.

We’ve all heard of the Westboro Baptist Church. They are the guys who protest at the funerals of U.S. soldiers blaming their deaths on gays in the military. They also hate Catholics, Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Lady Gaga, obviously President Obama, and they love using the word fag.

But the church is not really a church and their worldview is not limited to making public protests. The church is more like a family. Almost everyone is related to the head of the church, Fred Phelps. Their theology is a very chaste brand of Baptist style Christianity and is consistently conservative. Megan Phelps-Roper was considered the future of the church. A few weeks ago she left the church and won’t be going back. I learned that one of the keys to her successfully leaving the church was David Abitbol, otherwise known at @Jewlicious on Twitter. Abitbol used theological arguments to diffuse much of what Megan had been taught. She didn’t leave right away. She kept trying to revive her faith and believe as she always had. But she simply could not. So she left.

As an aside, how cool is that? Amazing work by Abitbol. He was outraged by the WBC so he did something about it. Very impressive. The rest of us were just outraged. The best I could muster was a 25 page paper trying to figure out a way they could be sued without protection from the 1st Amendment.

I found Megan on Facebook and requested to be her friend. She accepted and I thanked her for accepting the request and in the process called her a hero.

To me, she is a hero because she is “one who shows great courage” and also because she is “an object of extreme admiration.” Obviously it takes great courage to leave one’s family and entire life behind. But what about Megan is worthy of “extreme admiration?”

More to the point, should, as a religious orthodox Jew, be admiring someone who has forsaken their religion? Is there something hypocritical about admiring her for leaving her group and me sticking to mine?

I have two responses.

First, there is no comparison between the beliefs of the WBC and orthodox Judaism. The WBC puts the focus of their religion on hate. While orthodox Judaism does have some abhorrent beliefs, these are not core beliefs, nor are they central to observance today. If a person didn’t believe in the abhorrent parts of Judaism, no one would even know. Whereas in the WBC, those beliefs are the cornerstone of their religious observance. You can’t possibly avoid it.

More importantly, I think everyone should challenge their beliefs. It’s not easy to challenge one’s own beliefs and subject one’s views to scrutiny. Many of our great rabbis prohibited us from doing that. They felt it is too dangerous. We are strongly urged not to read books that are contradictory to our faith. They might be dangerous. But it’s also dangerous not to analyze one’s beliefs at all. It’s also dangerous if one expects every answer to be perfect. There are not always good answers. We need to be able to be comfortable without all the answers. But as our great rabbis taught us, we must know what to answer. Sometimes, the answer is “I don’t know.” Other times, the answer is “A is better than B, but A is not perfect.” Clearly, we are supposed to subjugate our beliefs to some testing so that we will have some answers.

Questions without any answers led Megan away from the WBC but Megan has not left religion. She simply left a hateful, hurtful, twisted brand of religion.

Megan challenged her beliefs and her beliefs failed to stand up to the most basic of questions. Walking away from one’s entire family because one is fairly certain their beliefs are false and harmful takes great bravery. It is admirable to do what one feels is right against the odds. If I was as convinced that what I was doing is wrong as Megan was convinced about her beliefs, I would hope to have as much courage as Megan. So yes, what she did was admirable. Challenging conventional wisdom and changing one’s beliefs is admirable. Doing it and acting on it is heroic.

The world would be a far greater place if more of us challenged conventional wisdom.

I hope we will hear more from Megan in the future. Hers is an important voice in the religious discourse of the 21st century.

]]> http://finkorswim.com/2013/03/12/the-heroism-of-megan-phelps-roper/feed/ 17 R’ Manis Friedman and Dov Hikind Explained http://finkorswim.com/2013/03/11/r-manis-friedman-and-dov-hikind-explained/ http://finkorswim.com/2013/03/11/r-manis-friedman-and-dov-hikind-explained/#comments Mon, 11 Mar 2013 07:01:50 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=6754 While I was away, two prominent public figures in the orthodox Jewish world embarrassed themselves. While I was away, two prominent public figures in the orthodox Jewish world embarrassed themselves.

R’ Manis Friedman compared getting raped to having diarrhea among other vomit inducing statements on abuse and Dov Hikind dressed up in blackface for Purim. We need not reconsider the incredible stupidity of both of these decisions. R’ Friedman fake apologized and then halfheartedly apologized later. Hikind didn’t apologize and then sort of apologized later.

But what happened? Why did they make such incredible faux pas?

Orthodox Jews live in insular communities. I think that a symptom of insularity is tunnel-vision. We see things the way that our small group of similarly minded people see something. We are infrequently exposed to alternative views when our peers and community reflect very similar values and ideas to our own.

Almost every orthodox Jew must deal with this at some point. Our schools, shuls, neighborhoods, little leagues, shops and stores, restaurants, and social gatherings are almost exclusively with people who are just like us. We even have our own media (along with secular media bans to boot!). Diversity is rare and not especially valued. This is not necessarily a bad thing. But there are going to be some side effects.

One side effect is that we may be completely unaware of how the rest of the civilized world feels about certain things.

Growing up, there was a racial slur that I heard very often. I guess in the back of my mind I knew it was not appropriate, but it was part of the vernacular of the social environment I was part of. One Shabbos evening, when I was a campus rabbi at USC, I had some students over and we were talking outside as they were about to leave. A name came up and I said something like “I thought they were [racial slur - (not the N word)]. The second I said it, I regretted it. I realized immediately that it was totally inappropriate. The reaction of my politically correct college student guests was enough to send me digging a grave beneath my feet. I apologized right away, but I should not have said it in the first place.

I am not excusing my behavior. However, it was new for me to be outside the comfort zone of my insular community. No one would have looked twice if I had said that word among our community. But now I was on the outside. It was trial by fire. Saying that word in the real world is wrong and everyone knows it.

Attending Law School was an amazing experience for me. I got to meet and talk with people from all sorts of backgrounds and with all kinds of different ideas. For the first time, I was engaging people with backgrounds that were different than my own as a peer. It changes you. It makes you a better person. For better or worse, most orthodox Jews don’t get that experience.

I am pretty sure that neither R’ Manis or Dov Hikind meant to be offensive or racist. They just had no idea how their ideas would sound or look to people on the outside. That’s a symptom of being surrounded by people who don’t interact with people on the outside.

If before he answered that question, I told R’ Friedman that sex abuse can be deadly, it can drive people to suicide, it can destroy one’s life, and he believed me, and he understood what I was saying, he would never have said what he said. But I presume that no one ever told him that. That’s becuase no one has been telling orthodox Jews the truth about sex abuse until very recently and very timidly at that. It’s getting better, but the degree of misinformation highly outweighs the appreciation of the truth about abuse in our communities. So it’s no surprise that R’ Friedman made comments that nearly caused me to lose my lunch and had me so upset for a few days. He didn’t know better. And how would he considering our insularity?

If before Purim, I told Dov Hikind that blackface was considered inappropriate in the 60′s and asked a few black people to explain why it bothers them so much, I am sure he would never would have dreamed of wearing blackface to a Purim party (and then saying “I would do it again”). I am pretty sure he had no idea and still doesn’t really get it. Whose fault is that? Partially his for not breaking out of his insular bubble, but in the end he is just a victim of his society that doesn’t really get it when it comes to minorities in general and black people in particular. In a community where no one bats an eyelash at racial slurs like the N word or the Yiddish slightly less offensive version, how in the world are we to expect any sensitivity toward racially inappropriate behavior?

Now I am not excusing R’ Friedman or Hikind for their deplorable actions. Ignorance is not bliss. I am saying that we as a community need to be more aware of what the rest of the world thinks. We need to realize that we are part of a global community now and we are going to be noticed. There is outside information that can help us and we should attempt to assimilate that information into our social structure. It’s not more frum to think that the Torah can cure sex abuse and it’s not more frum to say or do things that could be considered racist. It’s time to move on and learn from our advanced understanding of psychology and our politically sensitive society.

The same goes for how women are thought of and treated in the frum community. We are way behind the rest of the world in our approach to women. Is it really frum or necessary to keep those ancient notions that are rooted in social conventions and commonly held beliefs? I don’t think so.

We must hold tightly to what the Torah says and what Chazal require from us, but we must also integrate new knowledge and understanding that increases our sensitivity to others and our place in an international, cosmopolitan community. In this way, we can combine the best of what Torah offers with the best of what a global, sensitive society can offer.

At times like these, when Israel engages in a military operation and the world watches, two things are guaranteed: There will be biased or unfair reporting against Israel and there will be claims of anti-Semitism.

Anti-Semitism is an interesting thing. There certainly is precedent for Jew Hating and Jew Killing. Historically, this was an ideological position that was espoused by dominant religion against Jews. Whether it was Christians or Muslims, Jew hating was part of their religious beliefs. As the Western World became less fervently religious anti-Semitism spilled over into the secular world as well.

Along with anti-Semitism, dominant groups have long hated the “other”. Sometimes dominant groups hated each other. There are minorities in the United States of America, Land of the Free and Home of the Brave, that suffer discrimination on a regular basis.

I won’t argue whether there is something “different” about anti-Semitism or not in this post. I am not convinced that hating Jews is catergorically different than hating any other “different group”.

Rabbi Slifkin, who I love and with whom I have the pleasure of a personal friendship, argues in his recent post that anti-Semitism can be used as a source of inspiration to believe in God.

Here is the punchline:

Many readers are struggling with the fundamentals of faith. Is it rational to believe that there is something special about the Jews? That we are the chosen people? Aren’t we just yet another nation with delusions of grandeur?

I think that one can also draw faith, albeit in an odd way, from the blood libel, especially in its contemporary incarnation. In the entire global history of armed conflict, no nation has done remotely as much as Israel to minimize civilian (and even military) casualties amongst its enemies. Yet simultaneously, no nation receives remotely as much condemnation for killing civilians!

Is this normal?! Does this reflect a world in which there is nothing other than the order of nature?

Like I said, it’s somewhat odd to draw faith from something negative. But I find it to be an enormously powerful example of how there is something unique about the Jewish People. Overwhelming, bizarrely irrational global hatred; and yet we have survived, and returned to our homeland. Am Yisrael Chai!

I agree with Rabbi Slifkin that we should definitely try to draw faith from wherever we can draw it. I’ve written the same thing in a different context.

However, I was curious if Rabbi Slifkin actually meant that anti-Semitism is due to some supernatural cause or if it was simply irrational for people to hate Jews. His answer? The former. In other words, according to Rabbi Slifkin, the world’s irrational hatred for us is Divinely ordained. (I think that is what he was saying. I am not trying to put words in his mouth.)

While I said above that I won’t argue whether the phenomena of anti-Semitism is unique, I also will not argue that it is not irrational. But I will argue that just because it might be unique and irrational it does not imply that God is causing anti-Semitism.

The view of Maimonides (and some would argue Nachmanides) regarding God’s interaction with the world is that it is limited. It mostly manifests itself through Nature, as created by God. Miracles occur from time to time and bend or break the rules of nature. Not everyone agrees with this view of God’s interaction with the world. Certainly there are much more expansive views and in fact those views have taken hold in orthodox Judaism as mainstream opinions.

Rabbi Slifkin’s website is called Rationalist Judaism. While there is no such thing as pure Rationalism when it comes to Judaism, there are some basic principles to Rationalist Judaism. One of those principles, I believe, is “minimalism”. That is, when there is no need to rely on a supernatural phenomena or a fantastical interpretation, Rationalist Judaism does not do so. If we say that anti-Semitism is supernatural, I think we are saying that God interferes with nature and puts the anti-Semitism into the world. I think that is what it means when we say that anti-Semitism is Divine.

For the sake of argument let us presume that anti-Semitism is unique and irrational. I don’t believe that it implies Divinity. Hatred of other groups is also unique (maybe) and definitely irrational. Would we make the same claim and say that those minorities are persecuted by some Divine measure? I don’t think Rationalist Judaism would. Which leaves me to wonder, why would we do so when it comes to hatred or dehumanization of us Jews?

I don’t think we are forced to say that anti-Semitism implies a supernatural cause. Unless one already believes everything is caused supernaturally (a fine, traditional position) I don’t see why anti-Semitism should be different.

Lest you invoke “Halacha Yadua Eisav Soneh l’Yaakov” see this excellent article by Rabbi Adlerstein: Is Anti-Semitism Universal?

The NBA season kicked off last night. The league is 83% non-white player. That makes for an average of 2.55 white players per NBA team (15 player rosters).

So it is definitely notable when a team has more than 3 white players and it is extremely unusual for a team to have 10 white players out of its 15 total players. It might be unusual, but that is this year’s Minnesota Timberwolves. They are the whitest team in NBA recent memory.

This has created some problems for the team.

A civil rights leader has accused the team of signing white players to appeal to the dominantly white Minneapolis – St. Paul community. He thinks it is a ploy to get white fans to buy more tickets to see the games.

The team owner vehemently denies any such intent.

Not to mention that this tactic makes no sense. To make that assertion, one must assume that the white players are inferior and were chosen not for talent, but were chosen for their skin color despite their lack of talent. To assume that people are so racist that they will buy tickets to watch white players who are inferior to black players is quite an accusation. It means that not only are they racists, but they are idiots. It’s 2012. Americans want their teams to win more than they want them to look like they do. I’m very unimpressed with this civil rights activist’s claim.

The only real problem the team has experienced with so many white players is the choice of music in the locker room according to their lone black starting player, Brandon Roy.

But the latent issues that this accusation raises are worth addressing.

First of all, there are words used by fans and the media to describe players that vary depending on the player’s ethnicity. Black players are called athletic. White players are called smart. This is not meant to be insulting or racial. It just happens.

Second of all, the question of whether there is a subtle racism when assuming that white players are inferior to black players. While it may be true that more black players are better at basketball than white players, it seems to me that making assumptions based on race or color could be seen as insensitive. On the other hand, is playing probabilities and making snap judgments based on well established norms, racism?

I don’t think so.

But it’s a two way street. If another person feels like a comment being made towards them is racist, I don’t think the intent really matters. There is certainly merit and value to avoiding words and phrases that others find offensive. This applies whether it is reasonable or not for that person to be offended.

Then again, the street does go the other direction as well. It would be wonderful if we were all a little less sensitive. While it is understandable that some people are very touchy, especially when it comes to race, it would be wise for us all to raise our standard of what offends us.

Or to be excluded from conversations because we were not old enough?

I think that intellectually we have those memories in our collective psyche but we can’t really relate to children.

One of the more difficult roles that adults need to play in the lives of children is “Answerer of Questions”.

There are many types of questions and many approaches to answering their questions. Some questions relate to religion, others to society, others are just questions that we cannot answer because we simply don’t know the answer.

An article in this week’s NY Times Magazine addresses the concerns of one father as he and his son encounter stereotypes in books and movies. In just 60 years, we as a society have severely adjusted what we consider proper and appropriate depictions of minorities. Yet, some stereotypes persist and are still debated in 2012.

The article begins with the child asking why some pirates have a gorilla. The “gorilla” is not a gorilla. It is just a very ugly stereotype of an African male.

What do you answer your children when they ask about these ugly stereotypes? Read the article to see what that guy does.

But the article got me thinking about a few things as they relate to orthodox Judaism.

For starters, I think it is good for us to know that all kids have questions and many parents are placed in the uncomfortable position of answering them. It’s not just us.

Next, the obvious correlation to orthodox Judaism. In general, we teach our children a very midrashic form of Judaism. We also neglect to mention that there are other interpretations that are valid even within orthodox Judaism. So when our children ask questions and they challenge the wisdom they are being taught, we have ourselves a similar quandary. There are several options. We can defer until they get older. We can change the subject. We can answer the way we would answer an adult. We can reinforce the more fundamentalist view. We have to make the best choice for ourselves and our family.

But the most difficult issues arise when modern sensibilities feel betrayed by commandments in the Torah. I don’t need to be specific. But there are many instances where a child’s innocence can cause them to question the Divine Wisdom of God. What are we to say then? How do we explain to our children that God’s Book has different ideas about what is fair and just than we do?

There are really only three options. 1) We are wrong. God is right. Our minds are perverted by society and when our mind conflicts with God’s word, we must be wrong. Adjust your thinking accordingly. 2)We are right. God is wrong. Our society is smarter than the Author of this book that claims to be Divine. Toss the Book. 3) The word of God was just at one time. Society has changed and God’s will can change based on society’s adjustments.

Option 1 is easy. Fundamentalist Jews use this all the time and with varying degrees of success.

Option 2 is also easy. It not an acceptable approach in orthodox Judaism. Not one tiny bit.

Option 3 is complicated. Certainly, this is one part of the overall approach of the Reform and Conservative movements. This is not a sufficient reason for it to be wrong in the eyes of orthodox Jews, but it is a warning sign to many. The real question is not who subscribes to this belief, rather, is this belief an acceptable belief in certain circumstances?

Now I am not enough of an expert, by any means, to completely answer this question. However, I do have some preliminary thoughts. First of all, isn’t this the message of Avraham’s prayer for the Sedomites? I think it may be.

Further, some parts of Biblical Judaism have already been abrogated, either by the fact that we don’t have a Temple, or a Sanhedrin, or a Biblical Court. Other parts have been abrogated by the fact that we are not in a position to carry out the word of God. So what do we make of these? Are we to say that God’s will is that we should really be following these laws but we are anusim in these matters? Or do we say that this is fate? God’s will is that we should adapt over time and these laws should not be followed in 2012.

There are also cultural and social aspects of contemporary orthodox Judaism that may seem to be part of Judaism but are really just non-essential parts of our lives. Certainly, there are some things that fall into this category that may even be a mitzvah to reject. I will leave this category as a very general comment.

And then there are areas of Jewish law and thought that have not been touched or adjusted at all over time. Or have only been marginally adjusted. Admittedly, I don’t think any of those are explicitly found in the written Torah. But the Oral Law has generally been treated with the same reverence and reluctance for change. So returning to the original question, is Option 3 acceptable for orthodox Jews?

In some ways yes in other ways no. Is there a pattern or any rhyme or reason to when the answer is yes and when the answer is no? I don’t think so.

Most importantly, these questions are not just questions that our children ask, they are questions that we as adults ask. We can’t be afraid of the questions. We need to be confident in our answers or in our lack of answers. But either way, taking Option 1 or Option 2 ends the conversation. We don’t want to end the conversation. We want to continue the conversation that’s been taking place for 3000 years and will take place until the end of days.

First of all, an eruv is technically not a loophole. I explained in brief here: The Good Wife: Unorthodox, From a Rabbi / Law Student Perspective that an eruv is not a loophole. Here is what it is (and this is good for people who actually don’t know and people who need to explain to people who don’t know):

The prohibition against carrying on Shabbos is from a private domain to a public domain or a second private domain. One IS permitted to carry within their own home. One is permitted to carry within their own enclosed property (eg: a yard surrounded by a fence). One is permitted to carry from inside their home to their enclosed outdoor property as well. The function of the eruv (which unlike the not so funny joke during the clip does not mean loophole, it means mixture) is to mix / connect many domains into one LARGE private domain. All that is required is a SYMBOLIC enclosure that is halachically considered a wall. A wire running from two poles is sufficient to create a symbolic halachic wall.

Thus, when a wire surrounds a large area that has been designated as a single private domain, joining all the private domains therein into ONE domain, obviously carrying is permitted within that one domain. There are some requirements that need to be met for the large domain to include the private domains. If those requirements are met and there is a unification of many domains via a symbolic, halachic wall, one may carry within that domain exactly as they can carry from room to room within one’s own home.

Gerhart put the Stanford Cardinal football team on his shoulders and carried them to prominence for the first time since John Elway led them to prominence 25 years ago.

His college statistics are astounding: 1871 yards and 27 touchdowns last season. His combine numbers are impressive: 4.50 in the 40 yard dash and a 38 inch vertical leap.

Sounds like a sure-fire first round draft pick…

So why are scouts projecting Gerhart will be picked in the second round of the upcoming NFL Draft?

Gerhart thinks it may be due to the fact that he is white.

In the NFL, running backs are black. And Gerhart is white.

In his own words:

“One team I interviewed with asked me about being a white running back,” Gerhart says. “They asked if it made me feel entitled, or like I felt I was a poster child for white running backs. I said, ‘No, I’m just out there playing ball. I don’t think about that.’ I didn’t really know what to say.”

In the words of a scout:

“He’ll be a great second-round pickup for somebody, but I guarantee you if he was the exact same guy – but he was black – he’d go in the first round for sure,” the scout said. “You could make a case that he’s a Steven Jackson-type – doesn’t have blazing speed but he’s strong and powerful and versatile.”

Of course there are some legitimate concerns about Gerhart’s running style and durability and those are factors in his draft placement as well.

I can’t say whether this case is racism or not. Whether it is perception or reality is impossible to know. But either way I am rooting for Gerharts success. The more stereotypes that can be broken down the better our society will be.
