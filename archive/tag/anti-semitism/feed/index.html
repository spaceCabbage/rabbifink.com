]]> The week began with a salacious NY Post cover story and headline. Almost everyone I know felt that the headline was in bad taste and unnecessarily callous to a murder victim and his victim. What has been subject to debate is whether the headline and story were anti-Semitic. Some people are convinced that the headline was hateful and racist. Others see this is as a typical NY Post headline with no malicious intent. I agree with the latter.

Not to be outdone by the Post, the Yated Ne’eman ratcheted the hyperbole and histrionics to a whole new level. The people who think the cover story was anti-Semitic generally say that the insensitivity was indicative of a lack of respect to Jewish people. That Jewish blood is cheap. Or that it’s okay to make fun of Jews. Soft racism. But no one dared to compare the NY Post headline to the kind of real anti-Semitism of Ashkenazi history. No one actually thought that the headline was a harbinger of genocide or part of a general danger to Jewish people in America.

Enter the Yated.

In this op-ed, the Post headline editor seems to be compared to Lavan, Eisov, Nevuchadnetzar, Trotsky, Lenin & Stalin, Titus, Ferdinand, Isabella, Mussolini, and of course Hitler. The editorial is sure to make the distinction between America and the countries where we were slaughtered in Europe. But the relationship is noted and we are reminded that we will always be hated and we don’t really live here, we are actually like homeless people on a park bench.

The reactions to this editorial have ranged from the apocalyptic to approbatory. The immediate reaction of some people was to say that this is a disgraceful piece of writing that stomps on the graves of real victims of Jew hatred. It’s an opportunistic piece that so overstates its case that one might confuse it with an article in the Onion. Others see this as validation for their feelings. Many people are certain that we are hated and the NY Post front page was evidence and this editorial confirms their beliefs.

More than a few people asked me what I thought about the editorial. At first, I was outraged. I tend to err on the side of not saying things are anti-Semitism. So to see the extreme opposite of my own view so starkly presented was jarring and it bothered me a lot. But after another person emailed me about how much they enjoyed the editorial, I decided to read it again.

The reason I think the Post headline was not anti-Semitic or malicious is because it was a quote from an investigator of the murder. Taking the words in their context, all he meant was that there are a lot of suspects. But people jumped to the erroneous conclusion that the Post was condoning murder because they couldn’t be bothered to read the actual content of the article and think about what was actually being said. I didn’t want to do the same thing to the Yated Editorial. I think some of the people who are really upset by the editorial are doing a little bit of the same here. They see some words that validate their opinion, but I think it’s broader than that.

To me, the editorial was not actually saying that the Post was anti-Semitic or that its editor is comparable to the aforementioned murderer’s row. Sometimes we can see something or read something and it gives us a feeling or a sense of something that was triggered by what we experienced. If a victim of abuse sees someone who looks or even smells like their attacker, it can trigger fear or pain in the victim. It does not help to say to victim “don’t get upset, it’s a different person” because it’s not that the victim actually thinks it’s the same person. Rather, the victim is feeling trauma due to the association.

Similarly, one can read a book or watch a film and feel things that are inspired by the media that they are consuming. One can interpret art in a way that is completely opposite from the way it was intended. That doesn’t mean that the book, film, or art actually means the things that they feel. Rather, their reaction is colored by their life experiences and when combined with the media the result is a new thing unique to them.

I’m pretty sure that is what is intended by this editorial. It’s not saying that the NY Post cover story was anything like real anti-Semitism. Rather, when we see something that triggers our collective memory of anti-Semitism we feel something. It does not matter that two out of three journalists on the Stark story were Jewish or that the Post is likely the most pro-Israel secular paper in circulation. Of course it’s not anti-Semitism, but seeing those words reminds us of anti-Semitism. What we see and what we read can have an unintended affect on us.

The editorial says:

“I see mean,
Vicious,
Hatred,
Screaming loudly to the city and world.

I think back to
Lavan.
Eisov.
Nevuchadnetzar.
Trotsky, Lenin & Stalin.

All the way back in time.
Until this very day.
Who hasn’t wanted to see us dead and buried?

Jewish blood has always been cheap,
Nobody cared when they came after us.”

He’s writing about what he sees. Not what it says. He’s writing about where his thoughts take him after seeing something like this. He is remembering our bloody history. Not because this is that. It’s just because inevitably, our minds associate this kind of headline or the thinking of “who didn’t want him dead” with the most difficult of times. This is not one of those times, but to some f us it can feel like it is because we have been triggered by what was seen in the Post. He expresses skepticism at the friendship offered by our Gentile neighbors. Not because we know them to be liars, haters, or bigots. But because we’ve been traumatized before and forgive us for not forgetting the “friendships” of generations past.

So I can understand this kind of reaction. It’s not logical. It’s not even 100% serious. It’s just an expression of feelings that bubble to the surface whenever something can be seen as anti-Semitic enters the public eye. It’s okay to remind us that we are guests in this country. That’s the beauty of America. We are all guests here. That’s why we do so well here.

Still, I think it was a mistake to publish this editorial. I think more people will actually think that the Post editorial is being compared to real anti-Semitism. I think more people will attribute malicious intent to the Post. I think people will use the editorial as a confirmation that this is the beginning of the end for us in America.

We can do better. I think we can work on sorting through our triggers. We should try to see things for what they are and not allow our emotional associations carry the day. We should not be feeding the monster of seeing anti-Semitism in everything.

But this is what we get when a basic Jewish education includes elements of xenophobia and demonization of the outside. I am pretty sure that if we had less of that, we would have less Post Anti-Semitic Trauma Stress Disorder. We would do well to try to get past our PASTSD and we will be able to focus more on being good neighbors and citizens instead of dreading doomsday. A more sanguine outlook would be a breath of fresh air for our tormented people.

]]> Why do we focus so much on anti-Semitism? A recent article on Aish.com makes a decent point that the State of Israel did not solve anti-Semitism. Herzl hoped it would end Jew hatred. Clearly it did not.

But for some reason the writer, Rabbi Blech, emphasizes that hatred of Jews is unreasonable and defies logic. Well obviously. Hating anyone for no reason is by definition nonsensical. The odd implication seems to be that hatred of others makes sense and hatred of Jews does not.

I disagree. There is irrational hatred all over the world. Is it rational to hate people because of the color of skin? Is it rational to hate any of the persecuted minorities and ethnic groups? Of course not.

Yet around the world and over the course of history people have been persecuted and hated for being just slightly different. Whether they are different looking, different speaking, different thinking, different anything. There are plenty of excused people give to hate. None of them make a bit of sense. Hate is universal. Us Jewish people have not cornered the market on being hated.

I think what makes anti-Semitism unique is that Jews have lived all over the world and been a minority in so many places that we have managed to have more people in more places hate us for no reason than anyone else. Other hated people are more localized. Seems to me, that is a more logical explanation than to create a qualitative distinction between hating Jews and hating everyone else.

Rabbi Blech says the true answer to anti-Semitism is that “we persist in fulfilling our mission to serve as the world’s conscience until all of mankind learns to acknowledge the beautiful truths of our heritage.” I don’t think this is a complete answer. Hatred of Jews defies logic. Being the world’s conscience won’t fix stupid.

The more complete antidote to anti-Semitism and all hate is to preach love and tolerance, to find common ground with others, to create opportunities for people to learn more about one another and realize on their own that there is nothing rational about hating people for the simple fact that they are different.

There is no rule that states that people must hate one another in order for the world to spin on its axis. It can end and it will end. But let’s not limit ourselves to working on Jew hatred. All hate is the same and all hate can be fixed pretty easily.

]]> At times like these, when Israel engages in a military operation and the world watches, two things are guaranteed: There will be biased or unfair reporting against Israel and there will be claims of anti-Semitism.

Anti-Semitism is an interesting thing. There certainly is precedent for Jew Hating and Jew Killing. Historically, this was an ideological position that was espoused by dominant religion against Jews. Whether it was Christians or Muslims, Jew hating was part of their religious beliefs. As the Western World became less fervently religious anti-Semitism spilled over into the secular world as well.

Along with anti-Semitism, dominant groups have long hated the “other”. Sometimes dominant groups hated each other. There are minorities in the United States of America, Land of the Free and Home of the Brave, that suffer discrimination on a regular basis.

I won’t argue whether there is something “different” about anti-Semitism or not in this post. I am not convinced that hating Jews is catergorically different than hating any other “different group”.

Rabbi Slifkin, who I love and with whom I have the pleasure of a personal friendship, argues in his recent post that anti-Semitism can be used as a source of inspiration to believe in God.

Here is the punchline:

Many readers are struggling with the fundamentals of faith. Is it rational to believe that there is something special about the Jews? That we are the chosen people? Aren’t we just yet another nation with delusions of grandeur?

I think that one can also draw faith, albeit in an odd way, from the blood libel, especially in its contemporary incarnation. In the entire global history of armed conflict, no nation has done remotely as much as Israel to minimize civilian (and even military) casualties amongst its enemies. Yet simultaneously, no nation receives remotely as much condemnation for killing civilians!

Is this normal?! Does this reflect a world in which there is nothing other than the order of nature?

Like I said, it’s somewhat odd to draw faith from something negative. But I find it to be an enormously powerful example of how there is something unique about the Jewish People. Overwhelming, bizarrely irrational global hatred; and yet we have survived, and returned to our homeland. Am Yisrael Chai!

I agree with Rabbi Slifkin that we should definitely try to draw faith from wherever we can draw it. I’ve written the same thing in a different context.

However, I was curious if Rabbi Slifkin actually meant that anti-Semitism is due to some supernatural cause or if it was simply irrational for people to hate Jews. His answer? The former. In other words, according to Rabbi Slifkin, the world’s irrational hatred for us is Divinely ordained. (I think that is what he was saying. I am not trying to put words in his mouth.)

While I said above that I won’t argue whether the phenomena of anti-Semitism is unique, I also will not argue that it is not irrational. But I will argue that just because it might be unique and irrational it does not imply that God is causing anti-Semitism.

The view of Maimonides (and some would argue Nachmanides) regarding God’s interaction with the world is that it is limited. It mostly manifests itself through Nature, as created by God. Miracles occur from time to time and bend or break the rules of nature. Not everyone agrees with this view of God’s interaction with the world. Certainly there are much more expansive views and in fact those views have taken hold in orthodox Judaism as mainstream opinions.

Rabbi Slifkin’s website is called Rationalist Judaism. While there is no such thing as pure Rationalism when it comes to Judaism, there are some basic principles to Rationalist Judaism. One of those principles, I believe, is “minimalism”. That is, when there is no need to rely on a supernatural phenomena or a fantastical interpretation, Rationalist Judaism does not do so. If we say that anti-Semitism is supernatural, I think we are saying that God interferes with nature and puts the anti-Semitism into the world. I think that is what it means when we say that anti-Semitism is Divine.

For the sake of argument let us presume that anti-Semitism is unique and irrational. I don’t believe that it implies Divinity. Hatred of other groups is also unique (maybe) and definitely irrational. Would we make the same claim and say that those minorities are persecuted by some Divine measure? I don’t think Rationalist Judaism would. Which leaves me to wonder, why would we do so when it comes to hatred or dehumanization of us Jews?

I don’t think we are forced to say that anti-Semitism implies a supernatural cause. Unless one already believes everything is caused supernaturally (a fine, traditional position) I don’t see why anti-Semitism should be different.

]]> The Jewish Press is reporting that a very fancy Santa Monica hotel, the Shangri-La is being sued by a Jewish man. Our shul is just a couple miles south of the Shangri-La. The suit alleges that two years ago a group of young Jewish professionals were hosting an event at the hotel on behalf of Friends of the IDF. They claim that they had made all the proper arrangements with the hotel in advance and the hotel cooperated with them when they arrived. The hotel had a security and logistics meeting about the event.

Not too long after the group checked-in, the owner of the hotel, the daughter of a Pakistani Muslim real estate mogul, told the staff to remove the Jewish guests. They were easy to identify by their blue wristbands they were wearing in support of the IDF.

The hotel claims that the group did not make the necessary arrangements with the hotel in advance and thus were trespassing on hotel property when they were ousted. However, the attorney for the plaintiffs told the Jewish Press that the defendants were unable to provide any evidence to support their allegation that the group was trespassing. Further, the hotel owner was overheard telling hotel staff that her father would disown her if she did not have the Jews removed from the hotel.

While removing the Jews from the premises, the hotel staff were looking for Jewish guests and identifying them by their blue wristbands. The Jewish Press says this evokes the use of yellow stars to identify Jews during the Holocaust. An apt comparison, if a bit dramatic.

It is surprising that this case is proceeding to trial. I would have expected a quiet settlement. A trial will likely make this case a bit of a spectacle and that is unfortunate for all parties involved.

I hope they can be and will be accommodating and tolerant, but hatred runs deep and it is very difficult to deprogram people, especially if they are not willing to be reprogramed. The courts are equipped to handle this kind of behavior and it is far better than vigilantism that I have seen some other people promoting. I also strongly suggest that Jews not use this hotel in the future and urge all tolerant and loving non-Jewish people to avoid the Shangri-La in Santa Monica. A place that acts in this manner does not deserve the support or respect of the broader community.

Hopefully, the matter will be resolved fairly and the owners of the Shangri-La will learn an important lesson. Bigotry and hate are not tolerated in America. When you come here, you need to leave your prejudice and racism behind.

Most of all, I hope that the Jewish people can be a model of respect, tolerance, and love for all. We have been through the wringer over the last 2000 years. We have never been guests in a place as tolerant and multi-cultural as the United States of America. We, of all people, can appreciate the gift of American freedom and we should be ambassadors for that freedom. We should strive to teach tolerance and acceptance of others in our communities. It is an important value, and in our more insular communities it is in danger of being lost or forgotten without direct instruction.

This case is an embarrassment to Santa Monica, to Los Angeles, to California, and to the United States of America. Let us hope is it the last such case on American soil, and eventually the world.

]]> Tragedy struck the French Jewish community today. An unidentified gunman shot several people outside a Jewish school. When Jewish people are suffering anywhere in the world, Jews all over the rest of the world suffer with them. It is a great testament to our unity and sense of camaraderie that we can feel the pain of our Jewish brethren though we have never met.

Death, especially sudden death, inflicts a wound upon the survivors. When a fellow Jew is killed we are all survivors. For now, the pain is being channeled into grief and connection. Jews all over the world are expressing their solidarity and love for the victims, their families, and the entire French Jewish community.

When Jews are killed, another reasonable reaction is to immediately assume that anti-Semitism is a factor. This is turn, gets channeled into fervent Zionism, and sometimes translates into sympathy for movements like the JDL. I can understand these sentiments. With our collective history, it is absolutely a natural reaction.

With regard to this recent tragedy, there is no evidence of ant-Semitism. Complicating matters further is the forensic evidence that shows that other shootings in the area in the last few days were committed using the same weapon. The other shootings targeted French paratroopers of non-Jewish ethnicity. The modus operandi was also the same. A man on a motorcycle fired shots and then sped away.

To my untrained mind, it seems that the same man is the shooter. If that is the case, it is even harder to see anti-Semitism in today’s horrible tragedy.

I think it is slightly irresponsible to blame anti-Semitism when there is none. I understand that it is a natural reaction. But anti-Semitism cannot be presumed in any and every instance where Jewish people are killed. There may turn out to be anti-Semitism in this case. But at this point, I can’t see how that can be assumed.

Also, slightly cringeworthy is the “may God avenge their blood” sentiment. We can mourn and express pain without being bloodthirsty in our expressions.

In the meantime, let us mourn responsibly. Let us mourn these deaths as one. We are in a deep collective pain. Let us channel that pain to improving the world and increasing love and kindness in the world to properly avenge the death of our brothers and sister in France.

May their memories be for a blessing.

When the San Francisco ban on circumcision was first proposed months ago I wrote two posts defending one’s right to circumcise their son. One was from a legal perspective: Is a Ban on Circumcision Constitutional? Since that time, I have had several conversations in the comments on my blog as well as other Internet forums with the proponents of the ban. These folks call themselves Intactivists and they are a voice that needs to be reckoned with.

As Rabbi Adlerstein wrote in two places, the battle against circumcision is in essence a battle against religion. I don’t agree with every word of Rabbi Adlerstein’s essays but the fact remains that the reason Intactivists exist is because they believe religion is a myth. This is because a person who kills a rodef is not a killer nor is his act of attempting to kill the rodef is not considered a rodef. As an aside, a sexual or physical abuser is considered a halachic rodef and thus there is no prohibition of mesira in those situations. 2) If the moser is preventing a major communal disaster then there is no prohibition. 3) If it one’s job to inform, then mesira will not apply.

This is the most narrow view of when one is permitted to be moser that Rabbi Breitowitz mentions.

The Aruch Hashulchan says in a footnote to section 318 in Choshen Mishpat that in a benevolent and fair country where there is a justice system that does not unfairly imprison Jews, one is not guilty of mesira by informing the government of a fellow Jew’s crimes. He continues by saying “for example in wonderful Czarist Russia”. There is a question as to the seriousness of this footnote as he could not have possibly meant that Czarist Russia was fair and benevolent. The question is whether the entire footnote has value or if the entire thing was a false gesture of good faith to the Czar. It is not likely that the Aruch Hashulchan would add a deliberately misleading footnote and the reasoning of his footnote stands whether his country was wonderful or not. Thus, the Tzitz Eliezer holds that the Aruch Hashulchan means that wherever there is a “procedural justice” there is no mesira.

The third opinion is that of Rav Wosner. His approach integrates the rules of dina d’malchusa dina with mesira. His reasoning is that when the non-Jews follow the Noachide law of creating a set of laws it becomes a halachic basis for the Jew to obey those laws. Thus, he concludes that it cannot be possible to violate the prohibition of mesira if one is following the laws of their country. Since, he must follow the laws of that country his mesira is not against halacha. This does not mean one is required to be a moser, rather the reporting is not a violation of mesira.

It is necessary to define when dina d’milchusa dina constitutes to determine when mesira is not prohibited according to Rav Wosner. The Mechaber says that dina d’malchusa dina is limited to government interests. The Rama disagrees and says that it applies to anything that is designed to promote the well being of society. Most poskim agree with this definition which includes but is not limited to, criminal law, minimum wage laws, environmental laws and child labor laws.

In conclusion, mesira is a term bandied about to protect our own criminals. In reality, it is very difficult to pin down a halachic moser according to the Aruch Hashulchan or Rav Wosner and although it is possible according to Reb Moshe to be a moser it is still unlikely. We need to be honest with ourselves and stop hiding behind archaic halachic terms to justify false ideas.

WHAT IF GATES WERE AN UNRENOWNED JEWISH BLACK WOMAN?

Nathan Lewin

Posted Jul 29 2009

Soon after Cambridge police arrested and handcuffed Harvard professor Henry Louis Gates Jr. on a disorderly conduct charge, they realized the black man they wanted to prosecute was a renowned academic. As a result, he was released and the charges against him were dropped.

The ensuing public debate – in which Cambridge authorities now try to justify arresting a man because he refused to comply in his own home with police directions and deny that race had any influence on how the police reacted – suggests that if an ordinary black homeowner, rather than a Harvard professor, had been arrested under these circumstances, the criminal charges would not have been dropped so readily.

Consider the pending case of Rivka – a black, very dark-skinned, extremely devout Orthodox Jewish woman. Rivka is employed in New York, spends Shabbat in Washington, and her work frequently takes her on extended trips overseas. Being single and in her mid-fifties, she rented a 3-bedroom apartment on the West Side of Manhattan, and advertised on Craigslist for female roommates willing to accept short subleases. All applicants were informed by Rivka that the apartment was strictly kosher. A special page prescribing the kashrut rules was added to the lease that each roommate signed.

The rules were followed by a series of women Rivka accepted as roommates. In July 2007, a white blond-haired girl named Brooke answered Rivka’s ad and signed a five-month lease with the kashrut provisions. Brooke promptly violated the kashrut rules and admitted, in sworn testimony, that she brought nonkosher food into the apartment in knowing violation of her signed agreement. Rivka told Brooke she would have to leave.

Having e-mailed Brooke that she had arranged, at her – Rivka’s – expense, for a hotel room where Brooke could stay while she was looking for other accommodations, and believing that Brooke had agreed to have her belongings transported to the other location, Rivka began moving Brooke’s clothing to the hotel. Brooke arrived at the apartment unexpectedly and called the local precinct, reporting, “My roommate is currently stealing stuff out of my room.”

When Rivka returned after depositing Brooke’s belongings in the reserved hotel room, she found two policemen at the apartment with Brooke. The senior police officer (who is white), testified that Rivka showed them the lease and the e-mail she had sent to Brooke, and explained that she had moved Brooke’s belongings to the nearby hotel because she had an agreement with Brooke to do so. Rivka insisted to the officers that she wanted Brooke out of the apartment. The officers testified that Rivka interrupted her conversation with them to spend ten minutes in prayer.

Rivka then left the officers and retreated to her bedroom and locked her door. The officers demanded that she come out. On her cell phone, Rivka called a friend from the Georgetown Synagogue in Washington who had a law degree but was not in private practice. He told her to ask the officers for a warrant and for their precincts and badge numbers. The white policeman said in his testimony that he “did not recall” whether he gave his precinct and badge number.

When Rivka did not emerge voluntarily from her bedroom, the sergeant called the Emergency Services Unit – the police department’s riot squad. They came in full force, with more than one dozen policemen. They broke down the door to Rivka’s bedroom, dragged her into the hall and up against the wall at gunpoint, handcuffed her, and then took her downstairs to a waiting ambulance.

The ambulance drove to a nearby hospital. One of the officers tried to have her committed to the psychiatric unit. After an examination, the hospital said there was no basis to admit her. The police then took Rivka to the police precinct, where she was permitted one phone call. She called a person she had befriended while regularly attending Shabbat services at the Georgetown Synagogue, but the arresting police officer hung up the telephone in the middle of Rivka’s call.

Rivka was then taken to another hospital, where she was handcuffed to a bed. Next morning, she was returned to the precinct. At the end of the trial, he found Rivka guilty on both counts and fined her $500. Rivka is appealing.

Would the police have broken the bedroom door of a white gentile and charged her with crimes after being called to a dispute between her and a roommate? Is this another instance – as the Gates arrest is said to be – of injustice against blacks, aggravated by Rivka’s Jewish identity?
