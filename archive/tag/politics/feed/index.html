You can grin and bear it, move to another country, or secede from the Union. I have not heard of anyone actually getting up and leaving the United States of America in the wake of the election, and I am sure if someone does leave purely for political reasons we will hear about it. Though I wonder where a libertarian of conservative would enjoy living more than in these United States. Is there a country out there with more conservative / liberal balance than America? I don’t think so. But I could be wrong.

Of course, this is part of the blessing of America. There is always a balance. There are some states that are more liberal than others. There are some states with more conservative values than others. Put it all together and you get an odd mix of values, opinions, and policies that create a great country unlike any other.

The White House has a petition website where one can petition the government for just about anything. If you get 25,000 signatures on your petition, the government guarantees a response. There are now 20 petitions from 20 states requesting that the Federal government allow them to peacefully secede from the United States of America so that they can form their own little country.

Aside from the logistical problems of turning a state into a country and the oft ignored fact that the Federal government gives millions of dollars to the states, conservatives and liberals would be wise to oppose these shenanigans.

First of all, asking to secede after losing an election is the very definition of being a sore loser. It’s like the kid who takes his ball home after his team loses and doesn’t let the next team play.

But it is much more than that. Part of what America great is the diversity of its population. We are a nation of immigrants and though we Americans do not share a common ancestry or heritage we share common dreams. We pride ourselves on the great opportunity that awaits the huddled masses from wherever they come.

One of the great checks and balances built into our country is the political system where conservatives and liberals and everyone in between has a voice in government and ideally our government represents the best of both sides of the spectrum.

It would be a disaster if our country lost its great diversity and political balance due to secession. Now I know that these petitions are silly and reactionary. But I just think that if we would appreciate our country for what it is, we would not see attempts to secede after losing an election. We need to be proud of our system and if we want to change things but have been rebuffed we need to adjust strategy or tactics, not take our ball and run away.

I feel the same way about Jewish institutions. The Jewish people are also a nation of immigrants and huddled masses. Although in contrast to America, we do share common ancestry and heritage which makes us even more connected to one another. Part of what makes us great is our diversity. We are proud that there are so many different kinds of Jews. People to the left of us and people to the right of us may disagree on substance and style, but we are still brothers and sisters. For that reason alone we must do everything we can to identify with one another. We cannot afford secessions. Debate, dispute, disagreement, dialogue – those we can handle. But we cannot tolerate a group seceding from Judaism nor can we ostracize a group so much that we create a secession by default.

Our differences are our strength. It’s not easy to understand or empathize with people on the opposite of an ideology. But it is the sign of strength, not weakness, when we do.

]]> http://finkorswim.com/2012/11/13/the-last-gasp-of-the-election-secession-petitions-and-how-it-relates-to-judaism/feed/ 8 Torah Wisdom from Chayei Sarah: Counseling Grief Stricken Voters http://finkorswim.com/2012/11/08/torah-wisdom-from-chayei-sarah-counseling-grief-stricken-voters/ http://finkorswim.com/2012/11/08/torah-wisdom-from-chayei-sarah-counseling-grief-stricken-voters/#comments Thu, 08 Nov 2012 23:57:00 +0000 rabbifink http://finkorswim.com/?p=6440 President Barack Obama won his bid for reelection this week. Many people were very extremely emotionally invested in the outcome of the vote.

Obama’s greatest supporters wiped tears of joy from their eyes as the president addressed the nation in his victory speech. Mitt Romney’s greatest supporters were also drying their tears as they were informed of the results (captured in spectacular modern form on this Tumblr: Whitepeoplemourningromney).

In the old days, when your candidate lost, you grumbled at home, maybe made some comments around the water cooler at work, or maybe even penned an Op-Ed. But in 2012, when your candidate loses, the opportunities to publicly grieve are so much greater. Conservatives (and liberals would have done the same thing if Romney had won) took to social media expressing their distaste for the election results.

This means three things.

First, it means that people have an opportunity to vent, be heard, and create a conversation about the election in a public place. That’s great.

Second, it means that people can make one another hysterical with exaggerations, myths, or pure fabrications. Since anyone can write anything they want, misinformation can spread rather easily. Especially, when people are in a state of grief, they are particularly susceptible to hyperbole and over-dramatics.

Third, it means that people in a compromised state can say or do things that they will regret. But they are doing them publicly. This can make things awkward when they cool off.

I think that we need a little bit of advice on dealing with disappointment and grief. Fortunately, this week’s parsha is Chayei Sarah – the parsha where we learn about grief. Granted, Avraham was grieving for his beloved wife, but I am pretty sure some people loved Romney more than they loved their spouses, or at the very least, hate Obama enough to make it an apt comparison.

The Torah tells us that Sarah died and Avraham came to eulogize her and to cry for her. The next verse says that “Avraham rose up from the presence of his deceased.” (Genesis 23:3) Then Avraham went to negotiate a burial place for Sarah. The commentaries are bothered by the strange usage in verse 3. Why does the Torah say that Avraham “rose up”?

I once heard from Reb Yeruchem Levovitz that the Torah is telling us an important lesson in grief. So long as Avraham was grieving for his wife in private, it was acceptable, even laudable,  for his emotions to be on his sleeve. But once he began dealing with outsiders in a potential business deal, Avraham had to leave those public emotions behind. Avraham had to “rise up” from his mood of public grief and put on his game face so that he could deal with the realities of life. Outward displays of grief have a way of weighing on others and making interactions uncomfortable for other people.

Avraham was in complete control of his emotions and when it was time to face the world, he kept his emotions in check.

I think this is great advice for mourning conservatives. I empathize with people who feel terrible about the election. But I do not empathize with YouTube screeds, public plans to move to Australia, fear mongering, sore loser complaints, or any other anti-social reaction to the election. I think we all have a responsibility to act civilly toward each other whether we win or we lose, whether we are elated or depressed.

To be fair, I think this applies equally to public displays of schadenfreude. But I have seen far less of that than I have seen meltdowns.

My last piece of advice is not to become too emotionally invested in things that are out of one’s control. Politics and sports are two areas where people place a tremendous amount of emotional energy. We are setting ourselves up for disappointment if we expect to win every time. We won’t. Reasonable people will disagree. Your political opponents are not (all) idiots. There’s enough room for an amalgamation of conservative and liberal views in our political system.

I chuckled at liberals who mourned in 2004 when Bush won his second term and I chuckle today when conservatives proclaim Obama’s reelection the end of America.

Let’s learn the lesson of Avraham’s mourning. Pull yourself together and carry on with making a difference in the world.

Just over half of the country is happy with the results of the election. Just under half of the country is disappointed with the results of the election. No matter who won, some people would be happy and others would be sad. That’s just the reality of politics in America today.

However, there is one aspect of the election that should make everyone happy. By everyone, I mean whether you were rooting for Obama or Romney, you have the right to celebrate something.

Everyone should be celebrating with Nate Silver and fivethirtyeight.

To recap: Nate Silver confidently projected the outcome of the election nearly perfectly. This is not the first time he has done this. Silver has been blisteringly accurate in the two elections he has predicted. He came to politics by route of math and statistics, not policy and analysis. He was perfect last night as well.

While I am thrilled for Silver because he vindicated himself against the doubters (and the homophobes), I think his victory is a victory for all of us.

Silver got his start in analyzing baseball statistics using sabermetrics. He sold his model to the premier sabermetrics website and then moved to politics. His methodologies leave very little room for chance and are based on hard mathematical analysis. Silver removes subjectivity and does not rely on logical fallacies in his projections. This is despite entrenched ideas and established trends in analysis. People don’t like him because he seems like a magician. He somehow knows what will happen without tapping into the usual prediction patterns and totems. This is why pundits could not understand Silver’s projections. They are speaking a different language. The old media is using grunts and hand motions, while Silver is speaking the Queen’s English. They didn’t even realize that Silver was not making predictions, he was issuing projections.

By analogy, baseball has long been a sport which can be predicted with incredible accuracy using statistics. This methodology is known at sabermetrics. It was the cornerstone of the the now-famous Moneyball system of Billy Beane. The idea is that some advanced statistics are more valuable than the “eye-test” or traditional statistics like RBI or even batting average. Finding players who produce good advanced statistics but do not produce good traditional statistics and getting those players for cheaper salaries because they are undervalued in the cornerstone of Moneyball. For many years, traditionalists have resisted sabermetrics. They don’t understand the math. They are jocks, not nerds. But slowly, advanced statistics are going mainstream. We are seeing more baseball people relying on good data as opposed to outdated and less valuable information.

With Silver’s incredible projections, it is clear that advanced statistics are going mainstream. This is a good thing for all of us. It means breaking down our biases and tendencies to see things the same old way and using math to see things more accurately. It will help society, a lot.

We need to rely less on accepted wisdom and more on science and math.

One correlation that I have seen is that skeptics who challenge conventional wisdom using science and math also exist in religious environment. These people don’t want to settle for fallacious arguments or accepted norms just because they are accepted norms. This manifests itself in orthodox Judaism with Daas Torah and people who challenge Daas Torah. The Nate Silvers of the world don’t accept arguments because of Appeal to Authority. The argument needs to stand on its own merits. It can be debated whether this is good for orthodox Jews or bad for orthodox Jews. However, I think it is clear that with the advent of sabertmetrics and fiverthirtyeight style projections that fewer people will be willing to submit themselves to an ephemeral concept like Daas Torah.

As always this leaves us with a choice. Do we double down on Daas Torah? Do we adjust Daas Torah and its expectations? Do we shutter Daas Torah?

Whichever we choose, let us hope that it is the right choice for the sake of truth and God.

]]> http://finkorswim.com/2012/09/28/on-jewish-refugees-from-arab-lands/feed/ 7 Ideologies Don’t Turn People Into Killers http://finkorswim.com/2012/08/15/ideologies-dont-turn-people-into-killers/ http://finkorswim.com/2012/08/15/ideologies-dont-turn-people-into-killers/#comments Wed, 15 Aug 2012 21:09:22 +0000 rabbifink http://finkorswim.com/?p=6154 This summer has been a particularly violent summer in the USA. At least to my mind, there have been more reported acts of politicized public violence and mayhem than usual.

Aurora, the Sikh Temple, Texas A&M, and now the Family Research Council, are all very public examples of gunmen and violence.

As with everything in life, there are many prisms with which to view this violence and its social ramifications. I have taken an interest in the reactions after the shootings. It seems that the most important thing to establish is whether the gunman was liberal or conservative. The next thing to do is to show how liberalism or conservatism is the reason that the gunmen killed innocent people. The next step is to proclaim the ideology of the gunmen an ideology of violence and barbarism.

You’ll see this from both sides of the aisle.

It reminds me of a similar exercise in futilty in debates between believers and non-believers. That argument usually follows the very logical path of analysis that attributes historical and contemporary violence to fervent religious beliefs. Events like the Inquisition, Crusades, and  violence associated with Jihad are favorite examples used to prove this point.

In my opinion, all of these analyses are extremely superficial and contain numerous fallacies.

Systematic killing is not a relevant discussion to sporadic violence. There is a systematic killing in some places in the world. It so happens that those places are third world countries that do not value life in the same way more civilized countries value life. So comparing militants in the Sudan to a shooter at the FRC in DC is a complete non-sequitur. Violent outbursts in modern society are not the same kind of killings as systematic killings which were mostly done for the sake of power or position and they leveraged religion to accomplish those goals. But that is not our concern. It does not tell us anything about violent martyrs who kill for a cause expecting to die.

The reason people kill other people is very complex. There are many factors that contribute to a human being taking the life of another. Certainly one needs motivation and political position or religious beliefs may provide motivation. But at its core, taking the life of another is an extremely anti-social behavior. It takes a specific kind of person to kill another, even for the sake of one’s beliefs.

I don’t believe that an ideology can transform a normal functioning person into a sociopathic killer. The gap between “normal” and “murderer” is far too wide a gap to be crossed by adding only ideology. Further, let’s assume that less than .0001% of society murders other people. Meaning, for argument’s sake, one out of every one million people is a murderer. Personally, I think that is high, but let’s go with it. If religion or a specific political stance is so evil it causes murder, it is extremely ineffective at doing so. In other words, with so few murderers in society, it seems improbable to blame something as common as religion or political beliefs for murder.

Sure, many murderers hold extreme positions. But that is not what caused them to murder. Rather, the same thing that draws them to extremism is the thing that allows them to act in such an anti-social manner. To put it bluntly, people who kill other people are crazy. That is the starting point. Do crazy people believe in crazy things? Absolutely. But their beliefs are not what makes them crazy. Their beliefs are a symptom of their craziness, just as murder is a symptom of their craziness.

If there was no religion and if there were no politics, crazy people would still exist. And crazy people with access, opportunity, and motivation will sometimes kill other people.

So do me a favor. Please. Stop telling me about murderers who were socialists or liberals or libertarians or social conservatives or Christians, or Muslims, or Atheists, or Whatever. That is not relevant to their heinous murderous ways. People from all walks of life with all kinds of beliefs have been known to kill other people. More extreme groups will have more extreme people who have murderous tendencies. The beliefs don’t make them crazy, their craziness draws them to the beliefs.

The important thing that we should analyze about murderers is their mental health. We don’t need to eliminate religion or politics to stop murder in modern society, we need to deal with mental illness more aggressively and more effectively.

Similarly, most of the educators in our schools are not as extreme or unreasonable as the parents of the children in their schools. We cannot  allow the apologists and extremists to hijack or become the leaders of orthodox Judaism.

That being said, on the issue of abuse, the time has come for the Gedolim to really learn and appreciate what happens to victims of abuse. I honestly do not think they understand the gravity of the situation and perhaps if we educated them, their policy could change. If you would like to assist me or direct me in how to do this, please be in touch.

I am also talking about religious discourse. Arguments about the validity of religion, arguments over the rationality of atheism, debate within religions on the proper way to practice religion, debate within atheism as to what is the best way to be an atheist, take place on a regular basis with vigor and sometimes with surprising candor and logic.

Does anyone change their mind during these debates? Not really.

As an aside, I don’t think that means they are useless or a waste of time and energy. I think the goal of a conversation or debate is to discover the precise point of disagreement. This is useful because it helps the various sides understand exactly where the source of the argument lies. This can serve a very important role as it deflects the argument from becoming personal and it makes the argument less broad, creating  more space for agreement.

But the question that we have to answer is if no one changes their mind after a debate how do we develop our leanings? In other words, clearly it cannot be based on logic as the points are subjective and well argued points have little sway on the other side of the debate, so if arguments don’t impact our political leanings, how did our opinions form in the first place?

According to an article in the Guardian these leanings are deeply rooted in our psyches and are more nature than nurture, although nurture can effect nature. In other words, if you want to change your political beliefs, the only thing that you do is change your environment and who you associate with. If you want to change another person’s political beliefs, well, good luck. Our brains can even be wired for liberalism in some things and conservatism in other things. (That explains Jewish left wing modern orthodox republicans.)

All of this comes to a head when viewed through the prism of orthodox Judaism. There are liberal views and there are conservative views. Some people lean to the conservative views. Those are certainly the more popular views in orthodox Judaism over the last few decades. But what about the people who just don’t relate? The ones who find themselves gravitating towards liberal views within orthodox Judaism are often perceived as slackers, or looking for leniencies because they find the more conservative views too difficult or demanding. That may be true sometimes. I don’t think that is necessarily a bad thing or a criticism per se.

But I think that we can add this to the mix. Sometimes people are drawn to ideologies, even within their religion because of the way their brain is wired. Luckily, orthodox Judaism has legitimate conservative and liberal varieties of the religion. There is a place for everyone. We can’t squeeze out the people who are more comfortable with the less conservative views simply because the trend is to go in that direction. There needs too be room for all orthodox Jews in the spectrum to find meaning and satisfaction in orthodox Judaism and not feel marginalized by the dominant fundamentalists.

Nathan Lewin

Soon after Cambridge police arrested and handcuffed Harvard professor Henry Louis Gates Jr. on a disorderly conduct charge, they realized the black man they wanted to prosecute was a renowned academic. As a result, he was released and the charges against him were dropped.

The ensuing public debate – in which Cambridge authorities now try to justify arresting a man because he refused to comply in his own home with police directions and deny that race had any influence on how the police reacted – suggests that if an ordinary black homeowner, rather than a Harvard professor, had been arrested under these circumstances, the criminal charges would not have been dropped so readily.

Rivka is employed in New York, spends Shabbat in Washington, and her work frequently takes her on extended trips overseas. The white policeman said in his testimony that he “did not recall” whether he gave his precinct and badge number.
