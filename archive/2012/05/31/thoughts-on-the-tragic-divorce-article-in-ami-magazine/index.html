Thoughts on the Tragic Divorce Article in Ami Magazine

  • May 31, 2012
  • 165 comments
  • 0

I don’t normally read Ami Magazine. I don’t read any of the frummags on a regular basis. But once in a while someone sends me a particular article of interest. Last week’s Ami presented a short article that is worthy of more attention and discussion.

I suggest reading the entire article before this blog post. I have attached it to this post and you can read it by clicking here: Ami – Do Us Part.

To summarize: Uber-yeshiva student gets the uber-shidduch. She goes to school and takes out loans and neglects to tell him. He finds out and thinks her parents are paying. He thinks her parents will support them indefinitely. They don’t. Their troubles start with their second child around the same time he finds out that he and his wife are responsible for the student loans. Eventually things deteriorate, he needs to leave yeshiva, his rabbis are sad that he has to leave, they all thought he would learn full time, literally until he died, the couple gets divorced after their fourth child.

His lesson from the whole things seems to be that you should get the details of the financial arrangement with your in-laws before you agree to marry their daughter. If you think I am wrong about that being his lesson, I am open to other suggestions.

The article, I think unwittingly, displays several of the inherent flaws in the current yeshiva lifestyle.

Their marriage seemed like such a business arrangement. He would learn, forever, she would work and get money from her parents, their children would be raised by baby-sitters and this would continue forever. And ever. Forever, ever, ever, ever. It seems he never discussed this with his in-laws. It was expected without even a discussion. How does this become a default standard? No matter how amazing a talmudic scholar a 23 year old young man is capable of becoming with a lifetime dedicated to Torah study, if someone else is going to pay your bills forever isn’t a formal agreement and expression of gratitude the baseline minimum for such an arrangement?!

The wife is not completely off the hook here either. She basically took on a ton of debt without even mentioning it to her husband. Granted, he was not going to be working to pay off those bills, but to blindside her husband with an avalanche of bills and debt is inexcusable. Once again, a lack of communication. This time it was between husband and wife creating strife in the marriage. Sensing a pattern here?

Then we have the interesting fact that their marriage troubles began with two children in the family and they continued to procreate, no doubt as part of their devotion to God and religion, and doubled their family size before getting a divorce. The marital problems were readily apparent. The article says they fought often and they were miserable. So what were they thinking? Let’s just have as many kids as possible while our marriage falls apart? Let’s add more financial responsibilities while we can’t afford our current lifestyle? Let’s give more kids miserable or divorced parents? It makes no sense. Unless you consider that they probably never even discussed it. Again, lack of communication.

But this issue goes to one more point. The taboo subject of orthodox Jewish family planning. Is it responsible to have four children with extremely limited income, a husband who absolutely does not want to earn a living, and a set of miserable parents? Was there a rabbi in their life who was urging them to have kids? Was there a rabbi in their life who could have advised them that contraceptives might be a wise choice at a certain point? What happened here?

In my opinion, there needs to be an honest conversation between prospective brides and grooms with rabbis who can properly advise these young, wide-eyed,  soon to be married couples about family planning. Hopefully I will write a more comprehensive blog post about that subject sometime in the future. In the meantime, it is sufficient to say that not all rabbinic authorities in the charedi world are in agreement and most young couples are not as informed as they need be.

Finally, I think this young man needs an attitude adjustment. He acts like his life is ruined because he can’t stay in kollel. I really believe that he feels that way. But he needs to know that one can grow in Torah, yiras shamayim, and avodas Hashem outside the walls of the kollel. I don’t blame him for thinking otherwise as this is the underlying message of much of our Torah education prevalent in our yeshivos and Bais Yaakov’s today.

Case in point: A daughter of one of the biggest Torah scholars and ovdei Hashem that I know in the charedi world was awakened to this message in a rather shocking manner. Her seminary teacher advised the young women in her class that if they look for a shidduch and the guy wants to earn a living the young man is not a yarei shamayim and they should never consider it. She found that particularly offensive when she considered that her father works 9-5 every day and teaches a daf yomi b’iyun class and has been through shas b’iyun passing up promotions at work so he could keep his daily Torah regimen of several hours of learning all while earning a living. In my estimation, he learns more than the average kollel fellow. You can see why the seminary teacher’s statement would hurt this young woman.

That man should be the paradigm. That should be the ultimate goal. It should not feel like a failure for the uber-bochur to take his skills to the outside world and carry on his lifelong mission of acquiring Torah and growing in spirituality outside the walls of the kollel. All he needs to do is look for role models in contemporary frum society, and there are plenty, and he can affix his gaze on the myriad of Tannaim, Amoraim, Gaonim, Rishonim, and Achronim who balanced their Torah learning and scholarship with making a living. The way it was meant to be.
