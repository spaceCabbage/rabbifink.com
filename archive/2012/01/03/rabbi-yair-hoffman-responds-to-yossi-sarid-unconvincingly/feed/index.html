By the way, a cheresh is a deaf-mute.  He has no way of communicating.  A person who has never been able to communicate would indeed be exempt from mitzvos.  If someone can successfully teach that person to communicate, he is no longer a cheresh, because now he can be taught how to perform the mitzvos.

Avraham asked G-d if He would save S’dom for various amounts of tzaddikim.  The Bnot Tzelophehad asked a beit din if they could inherit.

If G-d doesn’t respond, and/or if the beit din upholds the halacha as is, then we have two options:

- return to our learning and find greater evidence that the halacha should change, and resubmit it.

-accept the halacha as is, whether or not we like it.

I don’t particularly like the laws of kashrut.  It’s annoying that I can’t go out to eat with my friends.  It’s cumbersome to have to pack a cooler full of food if I want to go on a road trip.  It’s frustrating that my non-frum and non-Jewish friends hesitate to eat out with me in the few kosher restaurants that are available, because the food there is so much more expensive than the non-kosher restaurants.

I don’t like the laws, and I don’t have to like them.  I keep them because I know that G-d has commanded me to keep them.  I don’t even try to weasel out of it, and falsely justify eating in a non-kosher restaurant, by saying “I’ll only eat vegetarian.”, or “these laws were for a different time.”

]]> By: Willbe http://finkorswim.com/2012/01/03/rabbi-yair-hoffman-responds-to-yossi-sarid-unconvincingly/#comment-6867 Willbe Wed, 04 Jan 2012 10:44:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=5004#comment-6867 The Talmuds make two kinds of statements.  Halachic statements (statements which define and influence actual Jewish law) and non-halachic statements.

The non-halachic statements reflect only the view of the world at that time (3rd and 4th century Israel and Babylon).  We are not obligated to these points of view.  Sometimes they offer timeless insights into the nature of humanity, while other times they reflect the norms of a society that is not ours.

The halachic statements of the Talmuds, specifically those that are cited when formulating our codes of practical Jewish Law (most notably the Shulchan Aruch) are binding upon us.  

Observant Jewish women today received advanced religious and secular educations.  They are doctors, lawyers, CEO’s, and other modes of career professionals.  They can remain single or get married.  They can have children, or not.  All the while, they remain members in good standing of any observant Jewish community.

They are unencumbered by the non-halachic statements regarding the nature of women.

However, these highly intelligent, educated, and successful women will not testify in a Jewish court, in such cases that our Law does not allow it.  These women do not seek to be counted in a minyan.  They will not get remarried, or have a sexual relationship with another man, if they are still married – even if that marriage has all but dissolved, but no get has been issued.

Contemporary observant Jewish women understand and respect halacha.  It is not only women who have restrictions.  A Kohen cannot attend his own grandfather’s funeral.  A mamzer is severly limited as to who he can marry.  This is our Law, and the fact that modern society has a different view does not exempt us from following our Law.

The G-d haters of the world will always pervert our Law and our practice of it.  ”…there is nothing new under the sun”  Ecclesiastes 1:9 

For the committed Jew, our challenge lies not with refutation of the G-d haters, but with our own communities, and ultimately, ourselves.  We must engage the world in which we live, while remaining faithful to our Law.
