Now; many Jews who are not shomrei mitzvot will say the same sentence at their weddings. (With observant couples, they DID intend to govern their marriage k’daas moshe v’yisrael at the time. Unfortunately, subsequent refusal to do so does not automatically end the marriage, although I wish that this were the case).

R’ Moshe Feinstein most famously ruled this way, to avoid classifying children of Jewish second marriages as mamzerim.

At a secular wedding (AFAIK), there is no explicit commitment made to stay secular, devoid of all religious practice.

That’s the difference.

]]> By: Mike_S2500 http://finkorswim.com/2013/05/21/on-second-thought-now-that-ive-seen-the-court-transcripts/#comment-12798 Wed, 22 May 2013 02:04:00 +0000 http://finkorswim.com/?p=7105#comment-12798 These cases are always very hard. A number (long enough that I still got newspapers in hard copy) of years ago, I awoke to find the divorce case of a woman we knew slightly on the front page of the Boston Globe, as it had been decided by the state’s highest court. In this case, mom was Jewish, dad wasn’t and both had become more religious both through the marriage and in the course of the divorce. The case at hand was not a custody battle, but whether the father would be permitted to take the kids to church when they were with him. The court ruled no, arguing that it was not in the best interest of the children, (which is the legal standard) and that it was in the best interest of the children to continue be raised Jewish, as the couple had originally begun. My sense is in general if the judge can’t see how the two parents’ lifestyles can be combined in a way that doesn’t leave the kids’ heads spinning, the parent who is making the more drastic change tends to lose out.

I think the notion of an “implied contract” is not really what wins, as the standard is almost always “best interest of the child”, but that continuity with how the child has been raised before the divorce carries a lot of weight as the divorce is a traumatic enough change without adding major changes in religion, type of education etc.

I have no idea how a judge is supposed to decide what is in the best interest of the child in the case of two divorcing parents of equal capability who are moving toward dramatically different lifestyles, whether that is religiously motivated or for other reasons. You kind of end up with a heart wrenching problem no matter what you decide. I can see the appeal of continuity as a major factor.
