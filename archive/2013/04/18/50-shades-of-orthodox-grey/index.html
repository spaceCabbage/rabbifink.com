50 Shades of Orthodox Grey

  • April 18, 2013
  • 21 comments
  • 0

The great Avital Chizhik published another excellent article in Haaretz. As she normally does, Avital captures the tension between modernity and orthodoxy spectacularly.

One part of the article illustrates a tendency to drift away from more rigid orthodox practice among twenty something single girls. Their sins are mostly benign, although some are more egregious than others. But they remain committed orthodox Jews so matter how they may slip from time to time. Isn’t that true for all of us?

In order to prevent such slippage, it is often suggested that marriage will “lock one in” to a certain level of observance. With two people working together to serve Hashem, it is more likely they will be able to stay on track. So we say “get married.”

This sentiment is echoed in the yeshiva world as well. Early marriage is idealized for many reasons. One of the reasons I have heard is that it prevents drifting.

There are several things to say about this.

First, I think it is true that marriage does make the possibility of drifting more remote. Marriage is such a big deal in orthodox Judaism. Getting a good shidduch is so important. I’ve often felt that one’s first 20 years are all just one big audition for marriage. It certainly would make it more difficult to change one’s views and practices when there is a second person who is counting on you to be the person they expected to marry and build a family with.

So it does work to an extent.

The second part of this is whether it is objectively good for marriage to prevent drifting. Sure we all want the maximum number of people to stay within orthodox Judaism. That is a given. So I think on a superficial level, marriage as a drifting antidote is good.

However, there are some negative consequences as well. By locking young people into a particular path, we create an environment that promotes sticking to one particular path. There are many paths that are acceptable ways to serve God within orthodox Judaism. When we create an expectation that one will not veer from their chosen path, we stifle opportunities for young men and women to find their own path in orthodox Judaism.

Further, some people will be so disgruntled in this system that it will cause collateral damage. Sometimes one parent cannot keep their end of the bargain. Other times one can feel so contstrained by the system that it becomes necessary to break completely free. Now we are looking at several harsh possibilities. Either marriages will fail, children will be confused by conflicting messages, or one person lives in silent agony and the other is oblivious. None of these are good options.

Stifling creativity is also bound to occur. When one’s path is set by the age of 23, it’s easy to simply follow the script. The Jewish people need people to think outside the box. We’ve got issues that need to be solved. We have so many things that we can improve. A lifestyle that is scripted makes it even more difficult to think out of the box and we all suffer.

But the real issue here is that orthodox Jewish marriage does indeed frown upon, or even make it nearly impossible for a couple to have divergent views on how to serve God. It’s hard enough to imagine a couple where one is modern Orthodox and the other is Chasidic. We can’t even imagine a couple where one is orthoprax and the other is a mystically inclined bitachon Jew. Marriages can fall apart when one partner changes their views. Schools, more often on the right, have a hard time with parents who may not be completely in sync with the school’s positions. There is an expectation that couples be compatible in every hashkafic way. Indeed,these are the elements of orthodox Judaism that prevent drifting. I wonder if we are doing this right.

Should we demand that couples see eye to eye on every important Jewish issue? Is this helpful or harmful to orthodox Judaism overall?

In my opinion, in general, orthodox Judaism must broaden its overall willingness for ambiguity. So many of us wear black and white and I can’t help but think it has affected our ability to see shades of grey. We are so concerned with who is “in” and who is “out”, what is acceptable and what is unacceptable, that we have trained ourselves to think in binary terms with regard to everything in orthodox Judaism. One area that is affected by this phenomena is in the area of emunah ubitachon. We have come to expect to feel everything with certainty. It doesn’t have to be that way. We have to adjust our expectations and be willing to live in the space between. We can be successful, thriving, orthodox Jews even if we find ourselves in the shades of grey.

This black or white mindset also has dire consequences for unity and mutual respect among orthodox Jews. But I think it’s affecting marriage more than anything else. There are a lot of couples who are told or who think that without compatible hashkafic views their marriage is doomed. This is not true. But it fits in with everything else we have come to expect with orthodox Jewish marriage.

Mutual respect and strong ground rules are more important that symmetrical hashkofos. Good parenting is more important than agreeing on yarmulka material, color, and size. The things we agree upon are more compelling than the things we may disagree upon. We can allow for differing views in a marriage. We should be willing to allow for differing views in our communities as well.

It might be in our best interest to consider less rigid expectations of marriage compatibility. We would potentially lose its current protection against drifting, but might stand to gain so much more. We might stand to gain increased creativity and exploration of improvements to orthodox Judaism. We might promote more tolerance among orthodox Jews, the Jewish people at large, and with our non-Jewish neighbors, colleagues, and friends as well. It could be that it’s not best for orthodox Judaism to send us along a very narrow track based on our choices in our 20’s. While we might have some more drifting after marriage, that could be a good thing in many instances. We also will create more potential marriage partners (shidduch crisis solution anyone?) and foster greater respect for views other than our own.

I think this could create a more viable and more vibrant orthodox Judaism for our next generation. At the very least, this is an important discussion to have. We should be challenging ourselves and our communities for innovative ideas and solutions to problems. This just might be one such solution.
