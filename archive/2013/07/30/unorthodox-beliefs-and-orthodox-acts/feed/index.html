But this post has nothing to do with Zev Farber, who does not assert that his beliefs would leave halakhah unchanged.

In my view, Judaism is essentially a wave that eternally sends the messages of God. However, in order to understand how to apply these messages we must understand how any given halacha or ideal functioned in any given society, particularly the original society, ancient Israel. When we understand this, we can “subtract” the societal elements to see the ideas in their relative purity and reapply them to our times. Waves, however, require continuity. For this reason, it is vital to understand how the Torah functioned in every generation since Moshe in order to do this right. This requires serious study and thought. Farber noticed, as the C movement did, that the logical conclusion of a critical approach to the Torah and treating a sociological study of the history of halakhah as inputs for future decisions, is something other than Orthodox halakhah. This paragraph fits their “Tradition and Change”.
